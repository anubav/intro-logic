3  Quantificational Logic

3.1 Introduction

In this course, we have studied two distinct systems of logic: syllogistic logic and propositional logic. The first allowed us to recognize as valid such arguments as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Every Chicago winter is cold\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm}Some Chicago winters are not snowy}}{\textrm{Not all seasons that are cold are snowy}} \]

Propositional logic, on the other hand, taught us how to recognize the validity of arguments such as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{The cat is either on the bed or under the bed\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} The cat is not on the bed}}{\textrm{The cat is under the bed}} \]

What about the following argument?

\[ \frac{\textrm{Everyone in the classroom is either in first or second grade\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Amy is in the classroom and she is not in first grade}}{\textrm{Amy is in second grade}} \]

While this seems like a valid argument, its validity turns not only on the significance of such syllogistic constructions as ‘every \(\ldots\) is —’, but also on the meaning of propositional operators, like ‘either \(\ldots\) or —’ and ‘not \(\ldots\)’. To formulate precise standards for argumentative validity that can apply to such arguments, we need a single system of logic that can comprehend both syllogistic and propositional modes of reasoning.

In this chapter, we will introduce such a system of logic. We will call this system ‘quantificational’ logic.1 In addition to mixed syllogistic-propositional reasoning, the language of quantificational logic will also extend syllogistic logic in another direction. In addition to allowing us to ascribe properties to individual objects, it will allow us to express relations that obtain between multiple objects, so that we can recognize as valid such arguments as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Anyone can go on the trip with a parent's approval\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Adam is Julia's parent}}{\textrm{If Adam gives Julia approval, she can go on the trip}} \]

This capacity for relational reasoning makes quantificational logic very powerful. It so powerful, in fact, that, with only minor emendations, it is capable of formalizing nearly every mathematical proof or deductive argument that you are ever likely to encounter.

3.2 The Syntax of Quantificational Logic

Before introducing the formal language of quantificational logic, let’s begin by considering a few specific arguments. By formalizing these arguments, we will get a rough sense for the various kinds of syntactic items that we will need to include in our language. Consider the argument:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Everyone in the classroom is either in first or second grade\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Amy is in the classroom and she is not in first grade}}{\textrm{Amy is in second grade}} \]

Take the second premise:

\[ \textrm{Amy is in the classroom and she is not in first grade} \]

This looks like a propositional compound, more specifically, it looks like a conjunction, and even more specifically a conjunction with a negated right conjunct. Thus, in the language of propositional logic, we might have formalized this sentence as follows:

\[C\wedge\neg F\]

where

\(C\): Amy is in the classroom

\(F\): Amy is in first grade

So a first observation is that to express propositional compounds, our formal language will need to include all the propositional connectives that we introduced in the context of propositional logic, namely:

\[ \neg, \wedge, \vee, \rightarrow, \leftrightarrow \]

The above formalization in the language of propositional logic is probably not going to suffice. To see why, consider the conclusion of the argument:

\[\textrm{Amy is in second grade}\]

How should we formalize this sentence? Since it is not a propositional compound, in the language of propositional logic it would simply be formalized by another atomic sentence, such as \(S\). But representing the second premise as \(C\wedge\neg F\) and the conclusion as \(S\) obscures the crucial relationship that obtains between the two, namely, that they are both sentences which say something about one and the same person, Amy. So both sentences must somehow refer to Amy. Now, if we want to refer to Amy in our formal language, we will need to give her a name. So, our formal language will also need to have names of individuals. For now, let’s use the letter ‘\(a\)’ as a name for Amy.

The second premise says two things about Amy. The first is that she is in the classroom and the second is that she is not in the first grade. When we say that Amy is in the classroom, we are ascribing to Amy a specific property. So, our language will need to include not only names for objects, but names for properties as well. Let \(C\) be a name for the property ‘… is in the classroom’. We want to assert that this property holds of Amy. To attribute this property to Amy, we will first write down a name of the property, followed by a name of Amy. In this way, the claim that Amy is in the classroom will be expressed:

\[ Ca \]

Let \(F\) name the property ‘… is in the first grade’. Then, all told, the second premise can be formalized as follows:

\[ Ca\wedge \neg Fa\]

While this rendering conveys the compound propositional structure just as clearly as the formalization of the sentence in propositional logic, it adds internal structure to the component sentences out of which this compound is built. Instead of treating these component sentences as structureless atoms, we have now indicated something about these sentences, namely, that they attribute properties to a specific individual, an individual who is referred to by name and who can be referred to again by that same name elsewhere in the argument. For example, letting \(S\) be the property ‘… is in second grade’, the conclusion of the argument can now be rendered: \[ Sa \]

Now, let’s look at the first premise of the argument:

\[ \textrm{Everyone in the classroom is either in first or second grade} \]

In the language of syllogistic logic, we might have formalized this sentence as follows:

\[C\textrm{\textbf{a}}E\]

where the term \(C\) denotes the property ‘… is in the classroom’ and \(E\) the property ‘… is either in first or second grade’. This sort of rendering is also unlikely to be adequate because it doesn’t expose the disjunctive structure of the predicate term, which plays an essential role in the reasoning.

Now, this sentence clearly says something about individuals, but it does so without mentioning anyone by name. Since the sentence doesn’t name anyone, let’s just imagine some arbitrary individual, and call this arbitrary individual \(x\). What does the sentence imply about \(x\)? It implies that if \(x\) is in the classroom, then \(x\) is either in first or second grade. Using the same notation introduced in our above formalization, we can express this fact about \(x\) as follows:

\[ Cx \rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx) \]

What the premise says is that the above claim is true not just of some particular individual, but of every individual. To convey this fact, we need a way of indicating that the claim is meant to be true regardless of who \(x\) stands for. We could indicate this as follows:

\[ \textrm{For all $x$: } Cx \rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx) \]

In the language of quantificational logic, in place of the prefix ‘For all \(x\): …:’ we write \(\forall x(\cdots)\). The upside-down ‘A’ written before the name \(x\) is called a quantifier. More specifically, it is called the universal quantifier. All told then, the first premise can be written:

\[\forall x(Cx\rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx))\]

To express this sentence, we needed to introduce two new elements into the language: the universal quantifier and the symbol \(x\). We will call the symbol \(x\) a variable. Syntactically, it will function very much like a name, but, as we shall see, semantically variables will have a different meaning.

The entire argument, formalized in the language of quantificational logic is:

\[ \forall x(Cx\rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx)),Ca\wedge \neg Fa\vdash Sa \]

We will later see how to assess the validity of this argument.

Now, let’s examine the second argument mentioned above:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Anyone can go on the trip with a parent's approval\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Adam is Julia's parent}}{\textrm{If Adam gives Julia approval, she can go on the trip}} \]

Consider the second premise:

\[ \textrm{Adam is Julia's parent} \]

This sentence says something not just about one individual, but about two, namely Adam and Julia. What does it say about them? It says that they stand in a certain relation to each other, in particular, that Adam is Julia’s parent. To capture this fact we will need to give a name to the two-place relation ‘… is a parent of —’. Let’s call this relation \(P\). To express the fact that Adam stands in this relation to Julia, we will first write down the name of the relation, then the names of Adam and Julia in that order. If ‘\(a\)’ names Adam and ‘\(j\)’ names Julia, then we can formalize the sentence as follows:

\[ Paj \]

Note here that the order in which the names appears matters. If we had instead written \(Pja\) this would mean that Julia is a parent of Adam. This, of course, is not the case since the relation of parent to child is asymmetrical. Why not write \(aRj\) to make the order a bit more perspicuous? We could, but our logic will later allow for relations between three or more things, and the prefix (as opposed to the infix) notation can be more straightforwardly adapted to handle such complex relations.

So now we see that, in addition to names for properties, we will also need to include in our language names for relations. Syntactically, names for relations will function very much like names for properties, only instead of applying to a single individual they will apply to two (or three, or possibly more) individuals. We will call both names for properties and names for relations, predicates. Predicates may be unary if they denote properties of individuals, or they may be binary if they denote two-place relations, or they may be ternary if they denote three-place relations, or quaternary, etc..

Now let’s look at the conclusion. Clearly, this is a conditional sentence. The antecedent of the conditional is ‘Adam gives Julia approval’. Again, this states that a certain relation obtains between Adam and Julia, namely, the relation ‘… gives approval to —’. Let’s use the binary predicate \(A\) to stand for this two-place relation. The consequent of the conditional is the sentence ‘Julia can go on the trip’. Here we are simply ascribing a property to Julia. Let \(T\) be the unary predicate that names the property ‘… can go on the trip’. Then the conclusion of the argument can be written: \[ Aaj\rightarrow Tj\]

Now for the first premise. This says something about individuals without naming any individual explicitly. If \(x\) and \(y\) are two arbitrary individuals, then the sentence implies that \(x\) can go on the trip, provided \(x\) gets the approval of \(y\) and \(y\) is a parent of \(x\). Let’s focus on the proviso first, which states that \(x\) must have a parent’s approval. We could write down this proviso as follows: \[Pyx \wedge Ayx\] Note that the proviso does not state that \(x\) needs the approval of all of his or her parents, just that \(x\) needs the approval of some one of them. The first stronger proviso, requiring approval from all parents, could be written: \[\forall y(Pyx \wedge Ayx)\] But what we need instead is a way to say something weaker like \[\textrm{For some $y$:} Pyx \wedge Ayx\] To say this, we will introduce a second quantifier, which we will call the existential quantifier and which we will denote by the symbol, \(\exists\). The proviso can thus be written:

\[\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\]

This says that \(x\) gets approval from some parent. What we want to say about \(x\) is that if \(x\) gets a parent’s approval, then \(x\) can go on the trip:

\[\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow T(x)\]

What the first premise says is that this is true not just of some \(x\), but of every \(x\). So, all told the premise can be written:

\[ \forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx) \]

The argument can thus be formalized:

\[ \forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx), Paj\vdash Aaj\rightarrow Tj \]

Now that we have a sense of the types of syntactic item needed to formalize arguments in quantificational logic, we can give a more precise specification of the language, which we shall call L\(_Q\). As usual, we begin by supplying an alphabet for the language.

The alphabet for L\(_Q\) includes an arbitrarily large stock of symbols that we will refer to as constants. The constant symbols will serve as our names for individual objects. In addition to constants, the language also includes an arbitrarily large stock of symbols that we will refer to as variables. Collectively, the constants and variables will be referred to as terms. We could, of course, use whatever symbols we like for the terms of the language, but we will generally adopt the convention of using lowercase letters from the beginning or middle of the Latin alphabet for constants, and lowercase letters from the end of the Latin alphabet for variables.

  • Terms:

    • Constants: \(a,b,c,...j,k,l,...\)

    • Variables: \(x,y,z,u,v,w,...\)

In addition to the terms, we will also need predicate symbols that can serve as names for properties and relations. Again, any symbols will do, but, by convention, we will typically use uppercase Latin letters for predicate symbols.

  • Predicates: \(A,B,C,\ldots,P,Q,R,\ldots\)

We will assume that every predicate in the language has associated with it a predetermined number, called its arity, which indicates how many objects are related by the property or relation it signifies. If the arity of a predicate is \(2\), say, then it is a binary predicate and names a two-place relation. If it is \(1\), then it is a unary predicate and names a property. When we wish to indicate that the predicate symbol \(P\) has arity \(n\), we will denote it by \(P^n\).

The language of quantificational logic also include the five propositional connectives:

  • Propositional Connectives: \(\neg, \wedge, \vee, \rightarrow, \leftrightarrow\)

and parentheses that can be used for delimiting their scope:

  • Parentheses: \((\,,\,)\)

Finally, the language also include two additional symbols, the universal and existential quantifiers:

  • Quantifiers: \(\forall, \exists\)

That’s it. If you go back to the arguments that we just formalized, you can verify for yourself that every symbol appearing in every sentence in these arguments falls into one of these five categories.

Next, we need to say which expressions—that is, which strings of symbols from the alphabet—constitute sentences of L\(_Q\). First we will define the notion of a formula of L\(_Q\).

Definition 3.1 The formulas of L\(_Q\) are defined inductively as follows:

  1. If \(P^n\) is a predicate and \(a_1,\ldots,a_n\) are terms, then: \[P a_1a_2\cdots a_n\] is a formula.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a formula, then \(\neg\varphi\) is a formula.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are formulas and \(\ast\) is a binary propositional connective, then \(\varphi\ast\psi\) is a formula.

  4. If \(\varphi\) is a formula and \(v\) is a variable, then \(\forall v(\varphi)\) is a formula.

  5. If \(\varphi\) is a formula and \(v\) is a variable, then \(\exists v(\varphi)\) is a formula.

Assuming that \(P\) is a unary predicate, \(R\) a binary predicate, and \(S\) a ternary predicate, the following expressions are all formulas of L\(_Q\):

\[ Pa \]

\[ Sxba\]

\[ Rax\leftrightarrow \neg Rbx\]

\[\forall x(Rxa\rightarrow Px)\]

\[\neg\exists y (Pz \wedge \forall z(Rxy\vee Pz))\]

\[\exists x(\forall y((Pa \wedge Px)\rightarrow Rxy))\]

Notice that, in some of these formulas, there are variables that do not occur in the scope of a corresponding quantifier. Take, for example, the formula \(Rax\leftrightarrow \neg Rbx\). In this formula \(x\) occurs, but it does not fall within the scope of either \(\forall x\) or \(\exists x\). In this case, we say that \(x\) occurs freely in the formula. Contrast this with the next formula:

\[\forall x(Rxa\rightarrow Px)\]

Ignoring the \(x\) attached to the quantifier, the other two occurrences of the variable \(x\) both occur within the (parenthetically delimited) scope of the quantifier \(\forall x\). In this case, we say that these occurrences of \(x\) are bound. Now, consider the formula:

\[\neg\exists y (Pz \wedge \forall z(Rxy\vee Pz))\]

Ignoring the variables attached to quantifiers, which variables in the formula are free and which bound? The first occurrence of the variable \(z\) is free, as is the only occurence of the variable \(x\), but the one occurrence of the variable \(y\) and the second occurrence of the variable \(z\) are both bound.

We now define the sentences of the language as follows

Definition 3.2 A sentence of L\(_Q\) is a formula in which no variable occurs freely.

Only sentences of the language can serve as the premises or conclusion of an argument, but non-sentential formulas are important as well. These so-called ‘open’ formulas effectively allow us to introduce into the language new properties and relations not named by the predicates of the language. Recall our attempt to formalize the claim:

\[ \textrm{Anyone can go on the trip if they are given a parent's approval}\]

To capture the relevant proviso, we began by writing down the following formula:

\[ Pyx \wedge Ayx \]

This is an open formula, not a sentence, since the variables \(x\) and \(y\) do not occur within the scope of any quantifiers. Because \(x\) and \(y\) occur freely in this formula, the formula in effect says something about the particular items to which they refer. What does it say about these items? It says that \(x\) and \(y\) stand in a certain relation to one another, namely, the relation of \(y\) being a parent of \(x\) who gives to \(x\) their approval. Notice that this relation is not named by any of the primitive predicates that we used in our formalization of the argument. Rather, it may be thought of as a newly defined binary relation (we will discuss how to apply this relation shortly).

To capture the proviso, we then said that \(x\) satisfies the required condition if there is some person who stands in this defined relation to \(x\). To express this fact, we applied the existential quantifier to the above formula, which resulted in the formula:

\[ \exists y(Pyx \wedge Ayx) \]

Notice that the variable \(y\) no longer occurs freely in the formula since it has been captured, or bound, by the quantifier \(\exists y\). Still this formula is not a sentence, since both occurrences of the variable \(x\) still occur freely in it. Thus, the formula still says something about \(x\). What does it say? It says that \(x\) has a certain property, namely the property of having some parent’s approval. This again can be thought of as a defined property, since the language as specified does not include a unary predicate that names this property directly. To see that this property no longer says anything about \(y\), note that we could define the same property by replacing every occurrence of \(y\) with a different variable, say \(z\):

\[ \exists z(Pzx \wedge Azx) \]

What does this formula say about \(x\)? The same thing as it did before the variable replacement, namely, that \(x\) has a parent who has given them approval. Thus, bound variables don’t really name anything, they are, so to speak, ‘dummy’ names. Like pronouns, they are useful, to ensure consistency of reference across a discursive context, as when we say:

\[ \textrm{There is something on the table, and \emph{it} wasn't there before.} \]

Now that we have defined the property of an individual having parental approval, the first premise in the argument is easy to formalize. It says that anyone who has that property can go on the trip.

\[ \forall x(\exists z(Pzx \wedge Azx)\rightarrow T(x)) \]

We have at last arrived at a sentence. All the variables are now bound, and so they are all just dummy names. This means that we can now ask whether the sentence is true or false without saying anything about what \(x\) and \(z\) stand for.

3.3 The Semantics of Quantificational Logic

3.3.1 The Significance of the Quantifiers

Having introduced the formal language L\(_Q\), we must now specify a semantics for this language which will allow us to determine which arguments are valid. As usual, such a semantics must tell us how to intepret the non-logical elements of the language. In the case of L\(_Q\), the non-logical elements are the constants and the predicates. How should we interpret these items?

Since constants are meant to function as names of individual objects, our interpretations must take place against the background of a fixed set, or domain, of objects. An interpretation must assign to each name an object in this set. What about the predicates? Unary predicates are meant to denote properties of objects, and, so following the extensional approach discuss in Chapter 1, we can interpret a unary predicate as a set of objects in the domain. This set of objects signifies the set of all objects possessing the property named by the predicate.

What about binary predicates? Binary predicates are meant to stand for two-place relations. We can represent such relations not by a set of objects in the domain, but instead by a set of ordered pairs of objects taken from the domain. The pairs in this set stand for those pairs of objects the first member of which stands in said relation to the second. If we wish to represent such a set of order pairs diagramatically, we can do by means of a collection of directed edges, or arrows. For example:

In this diagram, an ordered pair belongs to the extension of the relation depicted just in case an arrow points from the first member of the pair to the second. So, in set theoretic notation, the diagram represents the two-place relation whose extension is given by the set:

\[\{(a, b), (b, c), (c, b), (c, c), (b, d) \}\]

What about a ternary predicate symbol? Following along with the above approach, a ternary predicate symbol—denoting a three-place relation—will be represented by a set of ordered triples of objects taken from the domain. Three-place (or more) relations are a bit harder to depict diagrammatically, so it’s better to just use the set notation. The same approach can be extended so as to apply to predicates of higher arity. In general, an \(n\)-ary predicate will be interpreted as a set of ordered \(n\)-tuples of objects taken from the domain.

All told, then, we have the following definition of an interpretation of the language L\(_Q\):

Definition 3.3 Take as given a fixed set of objects, \(\Omega\), referred to as the domain. An interpretation of the language is a function \(\sigma\) such that:

  1. For each constant \(a\), \(\sigma(a)\in \Omega\) (we will assume that, for every \(x\in \Omega\), \(\sigma(a)=x\), for some constant \(a\)).

  2. For each predicate \(P^1\), \(\sigma(P)\subset \Omega\).

  3. For each predicate \(P^n\), \(\sigma(P)\) is a set of ordered \(n\)-tuples of objects in \(\Omega\), i.e.:

    \[\sigma(P)\subset\{(a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n): a_i\in \Omega\textrm{, for }i=1,\ldots,n\} \]

Now, that we know what an interpretation is, our semantics must tell us how to assess the truth or falsehood of sentences in a given interpretation. In specifying how we go from an intepretation to a truth valuation, we will have provided an account of the intended meanings of the logical elements of the syntax, most notably, the quantifiers.

The simplest sentences in the language are those of the form:

\[ Pa_1 a_2\cdots a_n\]

where \(P\) is an \(n\)-ary predicate symbol and \(a_1,\ldots,a_n\) are constants. In this case, the sentence should be true just in case the objects named by the constants stand in the specific \(n\)-place relation named by the predicate. This semantic rule for such elementary sentences can be summarized as follows. For any interpretation \(\sigma\):

\[ v^\sigma(Pa_1 a_2\cdots a_n) = \mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} (\sigma(a_1),\sigma(a_2),\ldots,\sigma(a_n))\in \sigma(P) \]

The propositional connectives will be given the same truth-functional meaning as was ascribed to them in Chapter 2. In particular, the truth values of propositional compounds will be determined by the truth values of their component sentences in accordance with the following semantic rules:

\[ v^\sigma(\neg\varphi) = \mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{F} \]

\[ v^\sigma(\varphi\wedge\psi) = \mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\textrm{ and }v^\sigma(\psi)=\mathbf{T} \]

\[ v^\sigma(\varphi\vee\psi) = \mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\textrm{ or }v^\sigma(\psi)=\mathbf{T}\textrm{ (or both)} \]

\[ v^\sigma(\varphi\rightarrow\psi) = \mathbf{F}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\textrm{ and }v^\sigma(\psi)=\mathbf{F} \]

\[ v^\sigma(\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi) = \mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi)=v^\sigma(\psi) \]

Lastly, we must explain how to assess the truth values of quantified sentences in a given interpretation. To do so, we must introduce one further piece of terminology. Let \(\varphi\) be the open formula:

\[\forall z(Rxz\rightarrow Py)\]

In this formula, the only variables that occur freely are the variables \(x\) and \(y\). To indicate this fact, we will denote the formula as follows:

\[ \varphi(x,y) \]

Note that this not a new formula. It is just another name for the formula given above, but one which carries with it additional information about which variables occur freely in it. In particular, this way of denoting the formula indicates that no variables other than \(x\) and \(y\) occur freely in \(\varphi\). As discussed above, the formula \(\varphi(x,y)\), having two free variables, can be thought of as a defined two-place relation. How do I assert that this relation holds of the objects named by the constants \(a\) and \(b\)? I do so by substituting \(a\) for every free occurrence of \(x\) in the formula, and substituting \(b\) for every free occurrence of \(y\). This results in the sentence:

\[\forall z(Raz\rightarrow Pb)\]

which we will denote by:

\[ \varphi(a,b) \]

We will term this process, whereby a formula is turned into a sentence by replacing the free occurrences of variables in it with constants, instantiation.

With this idea in hand, we can now can return to the question of how to assess the truth or falsehood of quantified sentences. Consider the sentence:

\[\forall x(\varphi)\]

Note that this may be a sentence despite the fact that the formula \(\varphi\) appearing in the scope of the quantifier is not itself a sentence. But if so, then it must be the case that the only variable occurring freely in the formula \(\varphi\) is \(x\), and so we can denote \(\varphi\) by \(\varphi(x)\). The universal quantifier means that every instantiation of this formula, that is every sentence of the form \(\varphi(a)\), expresses a true sentence. We can capture this fact, by the following semantic rule:

\[ v^\sigma(\forall x(\varphi))=\mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi(a))=\mathbf{T}\textrm{, for every constant $a$.} \]

In similar fashion, the existential quantifier can be interpreted as meaning that some instantiation of the formula to which the quantifier applies is a true sentence.

\[ v^\sigma(\exists x(\varphi))=\mathbf{T}\textrm{\quad iff \quad} v^\sigma(\varphi(a))=\mathbf{T}\textrm{, for some constant $a$.} \]

This completes our semantic specification of the truth conditions for the sentences of L\(_Q\). Let’s see how this semantics allows us to assess the validity of arguments. Consider the argument introduced in the previous section:

\[ \forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx), Paj\vdash Aaj\rightarrow Tj \]

Suppose that the first premise is true in some interpretation \(\sigma\), i.e.:

\[ v^\sigma(\forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx))=\mathbf{T}\]

In accordance with our above rule, this means that every instantiation of the formula:

\[\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx\]

in which \(x\) occurs freely, is true in the intepretation \(\sigma\). In particular:

\[ v^\sigma(\exists y(Pyj\wedge Ayj)\rightarrow Tj)=\mathbf{T} \]

The second premise says that \(Paj\) is true in \(\sigma\). To show that the truth of the conclusion follows, let’s suppose that \(Aaj\) is true and try to argue for the truth of \(Tj\). If \(Aaj\) is true, then:

\[ v^\sigma(Paj\wedge Aaj)=\mathbf{T}\]

Now, by our rule for existentially quantified sentencs, it follows that:

\[ v^\sigma(\exists y (Pyj\wedge Ayj))=\mathbf{T}\]

But since we have already seen that the conditional \(\exists y(Pyj\wedge Ayj)\rightarrow Tj\) is true, it follows that \(Tj\) is true as well. So the truth of the conclusion follows from the truth of the premises, i.e., the argument is valid.

Let’s now consider an invalid argument:

\[ \forall x(\exists y (Rxy))\vdash\exists x(\forall y (Rxy)) \]

To show that this argument is invalid, we must provide an interpretation under which the premise is true and the conclusion false. The premise says that everything stands in the relation \(R\) to something. The conclusion says that something stands in the relation \(R\) to everything. One interpretation of the relation \(R\) on which the first of these claims is true and the second false is the following:

In this diagram, every object points to something, but no object points to everything. In particular \(a\) does not point to itself and neither does \(b\). So, the premise is true but the conclusion is false. The argument is invalid.

3.3.2 Orders, Equivalences and Identity

Consider the following argument:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Sue is taller than James\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Sue is not taller than Bob}}{\textrm{James is not taller than Bob}} \]

Intuitively, this seems like a good argument, for the truth of the premises does seem to entail the truth of the conclusion. But suppose we formalize this argument in L\(_Q\) by introducing constants naming Sue (\(s\)), James (\(j\)), and Bob (\(b\)), and a binary predicate \(T\) that stands for the two-place relation ‘… is taller than —’. The argument would then be rendered as follows:

\[ Tsj, \neg Tsb\vdash\neg Tjb \]

As stated, this is not a valid argument, for it is easy to give an interpretation of the language in which the premises are true and the conclusion false. All that is required is to interpret \(T\) such that its extension includes the ordered pairs \((\textrm{Sue}, \textrm{James})\) and \((\textrm{James}, \textrm{Bob})\), but does not include the ordered pair \((\textrm{Sue}, \textrm{Bob})\).

Why then does the argument appear to be valid? The answer is that the relation ‘… is taller than —’ is not just any old relation. It is a special kind of relation, one which does not just relate arbitrary individuals, but relates them in a special sort of way. In praticular, it relates individuals by ordering them according to their height. We call relations like ‘… is taller than —’ ordering relations, or simply orders, for short. When you start looking for them, orders are easy to find. The relation ‘… is older than —’ is an order (by age), and the relation ‘… is to the left of —’ is an order (by position), and the relation ‘… is a proper subset of —’ is an order (by set-theoretic content).

To recognize the above argument as valid, we need to include among our assumptions the additional posit that the two place relation ‘… is taller than —’ is an order. How do we do so? It turns out that orders have certain special features that not all relations possess and which can be used to single them out. In particular, an order is any two place relation \(R\) which satisfies the following two properties:

Irreflexivity: Nothing stands in the relation \(R\) to itself.

Transitivity: If \(x\) stands in the relation \(R\) to \(y\), and \(y\) stands in the relation \(R\) to \(z\), then \(x\) stands in the relation \(R\) to \(z\).

To see that ‘… is taller than—’ is an order, it is enough to note that it is an irreflexive, transitive relation. Nobody, after all, is taller than himself, and if one person is taller than another, and that other person is taller than a third, then the first person is taller than the third. Note that both the conditions of irreflexivity and transitivity can be expressed in the language L\(_Q\). Applied to the binary predicate \(T\), we can state these conditions as follows:

Irreflexivity: \(\forall x(\neg Txx)\)

Transitivity: \(\forall x(\forall y(\forall z(Txy \wedge Tyz \rightarrow Txz)))\)

To stipulate that \(T\) is an order, we can simply add these two sentences to the argument as premises:

\[ \forall x(\neg Txx), \forall x(\forall y(\forall z(Txy \wedge Tyz \rightarrow Txz))), Tsj, \neg Tsb\vdash\neg Tjb \]

This argument is valid. To see why, suppose for contradiction that the premises are all true, but that the conclusion is false. If the conclusion is false it means that \(Tjb\) is true. Now, the premise \(Tsj\) is true as well. But since the transitivity condition is also true, by the truth conditions for universally quantified sentences, the following sentence is true:

\[Tsj\wedge Tjb\rightarrow Tsb\]

Hence, \(Tsb\) is true, which contradicts the assumption that the premise \(\neg Tsb\) is true. So, our original intuition that the argument is valid is born out, only now we see that that intuition was grounded in the unstated assumption that ‘… is taller than —’ is an order.

Here’s another example of a valid argument that relies on the ordering assumption:

Now, consider the following argument:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Sue is in the same grade as James\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Bob is in the same grade as James}}{\textrm{Sue is in the same grade as Bob}} \]

Again, this is not a valid argument if formulated as follows:

Note, however, that this relation is not an order, since it is reflexive: everyone, after all, is in the same grade as him or herself. Instead it is another kind of special relation, one which relates things by grouping them together in accordance with some shared property. These relations are called equivalence relations, or equivalences, for short.

The three properties which make the binary predicate \(G\) an equivalence relation are as follows:

Reflexivity: \(\forall x(\neg Gxx)\)

Symmetry: \(\forall x(\forall y(Gxy\rightarrow Gyx))\)

Transitivity: \(\forall x(\forall y(\forall z(Gxy \wedge Gyz \rightarrow Gxz)))\)

If we add these three relations as premises to the argument, then the argument becomes valid. To see why observes that, by symmetry and \(Gbj\), we have \(Gjb\). But by \(Gsj\) and \(Gjb\), we have \(Gsb\), by transitivity.

\(P(x)\leftrightarrow P(y)\)

There is one special equivalence relation that is so fundamental to our reasoning, that it is often treated as part of the logical syntax of the language. This is the relation ‘… is identical to —’, or the so-called identity relation. It is the relation that obtains between any object and itself, but fails to obtain between any object and any other. Because it so fundamental, we often give this relation a special name in the language, ‘=’. In the case of this one predicate, it is also standard to use the infix instead of the prefix notation. Thus, we write \(x = y\) in place of \(=xy\). Also, for the formula \(\neg x = y\), we adopt the shorthand \(x\neq y\).

When we say that identity is sometimes treated as a logical notion, what we mean is that its meaning is fixed across all interpretations. In particular, we stipulate that, in any interpretation, the extension of the binary predicate ‘\(=\)’ is just:

\[\{(a,a): a\in \Omega\}\]

With the help of the identity relation we can express claims not just about which objects have which properties or stand in which relations to each other, but how many objects have those properties or stand in those relations. For example, suppose we want to say there are exactly two objects that have the property \(P\). One natural idea would be to express this claim as follows:

\[ \exists x (\exists y (Px\wedge Py))\]

This says that there exists some \(x\) and there exists some \(y\) such that \(x\) has the property \(P\) and \(y\) has the property \(P\). But this doesn’t do the trick. For simply because we have used two different variables, \(x\) and \(y\), this does not ensure that these variables stand for different things. Hence, the above sentence would still be true if there was only one object in the domain with the property \(P\). What we need to add to the above sentence is the further condition that \(x\) and \(y\) are names of distinct objects. We can do this using the identity predicate as follows:

\[ \exists x (\exists y (Px\wedge Py \wedge x\neq y))\]

There is exactly one object in the universe with property \(P\). There are exactly two objects in the universe with property \(P\). Now this says that some \(x\) and there exists some \(y\) such that \(x\) has the property \(P\), and \(y\) has the property \(P\), and \(x\) and \(y\) are not identical. From this it does follow that there are two objects with the property \(P\). But this sentence, by itslef, does not rule out the possibility of there being more than two objects with this property. To rule out this possibility, we have to say, in addition to \(x\) and \(y\) being distinct objects with the property \(P\), any object which has this property is identical to either \(x\) or \(y\). Again, we can say this by means of the identity predicate:

\[ \exists x (\exists y ((Px\wedge Py \wedge x\neq y)\wedge \forall z(Pz\rightarrow(z=x\vee z=y)))) \]

3.4 Proofs in Quantificational Logic

3.4.1 Universal Introduction and Existential Elimination

Now that we have introduced a formal language for quantificational logic and given it a semantics, the next step is to say how we reason and give proofs in the language.

We will represent proofs in quantificational logic using the same Fitch-style notation for nested sequences that we employed in the context of syllogistic and propositional logic. Many of the rules of our proof system will be rules that we have already seen and used. In fact, our system will inherit all of the rules that we introduced for reasoning in propositional logic. Thus, recall, were the elimination and introduction rules for the propositional connectives:

Negation:

(\(\neg\)E): From the sentence \(\neg\neg\varphi\), you may infer the sentence \(\varphi\).

(\(\neg\)I): If from the hypothesis \(\varphi\) you can derive \(\psi\) and \(\neg\psi\), you may infer the sentence \(\neg\varphi\).

Conditional:

(\(\rightarrow\)E): From \(\varphi\) and \(\varphi\rightarrow\psi\), you may infer \(\psi\).

(\(\rightarrow\)I): If from the hypothesis \(\varphi\) you can derive \(\psi\), you may infer the sentence \(\varphi\rightarrow\psi\).

Conjunction:

(\(\wedge\)E): From \(\varphi\wedge\psi\), you may infer either the sentence \(\varphi\) or the sentence \(\psi\).

(\(\wedge\)I): From \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\), you may infer the sentence \(\varphi\wedge\psi\).

Disjunction:

(\(\vee\)E): If from the hypothesis \(\varphi\) you can derive \(\chi\) and from the hypothesis \(\psi\) you can derive \(\chi\), then from \(\varphi\vee\psi\) you may infer the sentence \(\chi\).

(\(\vee\)I): From either \(\varphi\) or \(\psi\), you may infer the sentence \(\varphi\vee\psi\).

Biconditional:

(\(\leftrightarrow\)E): From \(\varphi\) and \(\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi\), you may infer \(\psi\), and from \(\psi\) and \(\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi\), you may infer \(\varphi\).

(\(\leftrightarrow\)I): If from the hypothesis \(\varphi\) you can derive \(\psi\) and from the hypothesis \(\psi\) you can derive \(\varphi\), then you may infer \(\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi\).

In addition these rules we will also add introduction and elimination rules for the universal and existential quantifiers. The simplest of these rules are the rules of universal elimination (\(\forall\)E) and existential introduction (\(\exists\)I). The rule of universal elimination says that from a sentence of the form \(\forall x(\varphi)\), we may infer any instantiation of the formula that is quantified over, namely, \(\varphi(x)\). Thus, the rule can be stated as follows:

(\(\forall\)E): Let \(x\) be any variable and let \(\varphi(x)\) any open formula in which \(x\) occurs freely. From \(\forall x(\varphi)\), you may infer \(\varphi(a)\), for any constant \(a\).

Consider the argument:

\[ Pa, \forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx)\vdash Qa \]

Here is a proof:

\(1\) \(Pa\) Premise
\(2\) \(\forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx)\) Premise
\(3\) \(Pa\rightarrow Qa\) \(\forall\)E: \(2\)
\(4\) \(Qa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(1, 3\)

We apply the rule of universal elimination to get from line 2 in the proof to line 3. In doing so, we instantiate the open formula \(Px\rightarrow Qx\) by replacing every free occurrence of \(x\) in the formula with the constant \(a\).

Here is a proof of the first argument introduced at the beginning of this chapter:

\[ \forall x(Cx\rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx)),Ca\wedge \neg Fa\vdash Sa \]

\(1\) \(\forall x(Cx\rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx))\) Premise
\(2\) \(Ca\rightarrow (Fa\vee Sa)\) \(\forall\)E: \(1\)
\(3\) \(Ca\wedge \neg Fa\) Premise
\(4\) \(Ca\) \(\wedge\)E: \(3\)
\(5\) \(\neg Fa\) \(\wedge\)E: \(3\)
\(6\) \(Fa \vee Sa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(3, 4\)
\(7\) \(Sa\) Hypothesis
\(8\) \(Fa\) Hypothesis
\(9\) \(\neg Sa\) Hypothesis
\(10\) \(Fa\) Reiteration: \(8\)
\(11\) \(\neg Fa\) Reiteration: \(5\)
\(12\) \(\neg\neg Sa\) \(\neg\)I: \(10, 11\)
\(13\) \(Sa\) \(\neg\)E: \(12\)
\(14\) \(Sa\) \(\vee\)E: \(6, 7, 13\)

Now for the rule of existential introduction (\(\exists\) I). This rule works like the reverse of universal elimination. We infer from an instantiation \(\varphi(a)\) of an open formula \(\varphi(x)\), the existentially quantified sentence \(\exists x\varphi(x)\).

(\(\exists\)I): Let \(x\) be any variable and let \(\varphi(x)\) be any open formula in which \(x\) occurs freely. For any constant \(a\), from \(\varphi(a)\), you may infer \(\exists x(\varphi)\).

The proof of the following argument illustrates how this rule can be applied:

\[ Pa\vdash\exists y(Py\vee Qy) \]

\(1\) \(Pa\) Premise
\(2\) \(Pa \vee Qa\) \(\vee\)I: \(1\)
\(3\) \(\exists y(Py\vee Qy)\) \(\exists\)I: \(2\)

In the step from line 2 to 3, we use the rule (\(\exists\)I) to infer from the formula \(\varphi(a)\), where \(\varphi(y)\) is the open formula \(Py \vee Qy\), the existential sentence \(\exists y(Py\vee Qy)\). Now here is a proof of the second argument introduced at the beginning of the chapter:

\[ \forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx), Paj\vdash Aaj\rightarrow Tj \]

\(1\) \(\forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow Tx)\) Premise
\(2\) \(\exists y(Pyj\wedge Ayj)\rightarrow Tj\) \(\forall\)E: \(1\)
\(3\) \(Aaj\) Hypothesis
\(4\) \(Paj\) Premise
\(5\) \(Paj \wedge Aaj\) \(\wedge\)I: \(3, 4\)
\(6\) \(\exists y(Pyj \wedge Ayj)\) \(\exists\)I: \(5\)
\(7\) \(\exists y(Pyj\wedge Ayj)\rightarrow Tj\) Reiteration: \(2\)
\(8\) \(Tj\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(6, 7\)
\(9\) \(Aaj\rightarrow Tj\) \(\rightarrow\)I: \(8\)

3.4.2 Universal Introduction and Existential Elimination

Let’s now turn to the rule of universal introduction (\(\forall\)I). At first blush, it might seem difficult to imagine how we can prove a universal statement. After all, universal statements say something about everything in the domain, so to establish a universal claim such as \(\forall x(\varphi)\) one instance at a time would require us to first prove all of the infinitely many claims:

\[ \varphi(a), \varphi(b), \varphi(c), \ldots \]

Who has time for that? The rule universal introduction is based on the idea that we can shortcut this interminable procedure, by instead proving that the claim \(\varphi\) holds for just one individual object, but one object which, for all we know, may be anything.

Here is the idea, suppose that we have proven that \(\varphi(a)\), for some constant \(a\). And now suppose that looking back at the proof, what we find is that the constant \(a\) is nowhere mentioned in any of the premises appealed to in the proof. Nor does \(a\) occur in any of the undischarged hypotheses that are in effect at the point at which \(\varphi(a)\) is established. Then, since none of our substantive assumptions—our premises or hypothesis—put any constraints on what the object the constant \(a\) refers to, and yet nonetheless we have still been able to prove that \(\varphi(x)\) holds of \(a\), then it must be the case that \(\varphi(x)\) would hold for any x, whatsoever; that is, it must be the case that \(\forall x(\varphi)\). Here is the rule that codifies this sort of reasoning:

(\(\forall\)I): Let \(x\) be any variable and let \(\varphi(x)\) be any open formula in which \(x\) occurs freely. From \(\varphi(a)\), where \(a\) is any constant that does not occur in any premise or undischarged hypothesis, one may infer \(\forall x(\varphi)\).

Here is an example of how the rule can be applied:

\[ \forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx), \forall x Px\vdash\forall x Qx \]

\(1\) \(\forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx)\) Premise
\(2\) \(Pa\rightarrow Qa\) \(\forall\)E: \(1\)
\(3\) \(\forall x(Px)\) Premise
\(4\) \(Pa\) \(\forall\)E: \(3\)
\(5\) \(Qa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(2, 4\)
\(6\) \(\forall x(Qx)\) \(\forall\)I: \(5\)

The rule of universal introduction is appealed to in the final step of the proof in order to infer \(\forall x(Qx)\) from \(Qa\). Why is this inference justified? How can we infer that everything has the property \(Q\) from the fact that just one thing, \(a\), has it? The answer is that the object named by the constant \(a\), as it appears in line 5, serves the role of an arbitrary object. If you look at the premises appealed to in the argument, they say nothing at all about \(a\). So, as far as the premises are concerned, \(a\) could stand for anything. If, despite this indeterminacy of reference, we can still establish that \(Qa\), it can only be because everything has the property \(Q\).

It may be instructive, at this point, to consider an incorrect application of the rule.

\[ \forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx), Pa\vdash\forall x Qx \]

\(1\) \(\forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx)\) Premise
\(2\) \(Pa\rightarrow Qa\) \(\forall\)E: \(1\)
\(4\) \(Pa\) Premise
\(5\) \(Qa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(2, 4\)
\(6\) \(\forall x(Qx)\) \(\forall\)I: \(5 \qquad\color{red}\textrm{No!}\)

Here, we are not allowed to generalize from \(Qa\) to \(\forall x(Qx)\). This is because, as referred to in line 5, \(a\) is not just any object. It is a particular object that the second premise tells us possesses the property \(P\). It is this fact about \(a\) that allows us to infer that \(Qa\) and so it would be wrong to take this result and generalize to all objects in the domain.

The following argument gives another example of how the rule of universal introduction can be applied:

\[ \neg \exists Px\vdash\forall x\neg Px \]

\(1\) \(Pa\) Hypothesis
\(2\) \(\exists x Px\) \(\exists\)I: \(1\)
\(3\) \(\neg\exists x Px\) Premise
\(4\) \(\neg Pa\) \(\neg\)I: \(2, 3\)
\(5\) \(\forall x \neg Px\) \(\forall\)I: \(4\)

Note here that while the constant \(a\) appears in the hypothesis in line 1, at the point at which the rule \(\forall I\) is applied to it in line 4, that assumption has been discharged. And so this is a correct application of the rule.

Now, let’s turn to the last of our rules, existential elimination. In the elimination of existentials, we are faced with another problem. How can we infer anything from an existential claim, such as \(\exists x \varphi(x)\) without knowing which object bears witness to the claim? Here the idea will be that we can give a name to this witness, and reason as if it names one of the objects whose existence is attested to by the existential claim, provided that the name is a ‘new’ name that has not yet appeared in the proof, and provided that we do not draw any substantive conclusions about the object so named. In our proofs, we will implement this idea by means of the following rule:

(\(\exists\)E): Let \(x\) be any variable and let \(\varphi(x)\) be any open formula in which \(x\) occurs freely. If from the hypothesis \(\varphi(a)\), where \(a\) is a new constant not appearing previously in the proof, you can derive a sentence \(\psi\) which does not contain the constant \(a\), then from \(\exists x(\varphi)\) you may infer the sentence \(\psi\).

Here is an example of how this rule can be applied:

\[ \forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx), \exists x(Px \wedge Rx)\vdash\exists x(Qx\wedge Rx) \]

\(1\) \(\exists x(Px\wedge Rx)\) Premise
\(2\) \(Pa \wedge Ra\) Hypothesis
\(3\) \(\forall x (Px \rightarrow Qx)\) Premise
\(4\) \(Pa \rightarrow Qa\) \(\forall\)E: \(3\)
\(5\) \(Pa\) \(\wedge\)E: \(2\)
\(6\) \(Qa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(4, 5\)
\(7\) \(Ra\) \(\wedge\)E: \(2\)
\(8\) \(Qa \wedge Ra\) \(\wedge\)I: \(6, 7\)
\(9\) \(\exists x(Qx \wedge Rx)\) \(\exists\)I: \(8\)
\(10\) \(\exists x(Qx\wedge Rx)\) \(\exists\)E: \(1, 9\)

We introduce the constant symbol \(a\) in the hypothesis that initiates the subproof in line 2 as a witness to the existential claim appearing in line 1. In doing so, it is important that \(a\) is a new name that does not occur in any of the premises or undischarged hypotheses or previous lines of the proof. We then reason from this instance of the existential to the desired claim, at which point we discharge the assumption and write down the sentence that we have proved, by appeal to the initial existential claim alone.

It may be useful again to consider a few misapplications of the rule. First suppose that we tried to discharge the hypothesis at line \(8\) instead of line \(9\) to establish the validity of the following argument:

\[ \forall x(Px\rightarrow Qx), \exists x(Px \wedge Rx)\vdash Qa\wedge Ra \]

\(1\) \(\exists x(Px\wedge Rx)\) Premise
\(2\) \(Pa \wedge Ra\) Hypothesis
\(3\) \(\forall x (Px \rightarrow Qx)\) Premise
\(4\) \(Pa \rightarrow Qa\) \(\forall\)E: \(3\)
\(5\) \(Pa\) \(\wedge\)E: \(2\)
\(6\) \(Qa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(4, 5\)
\(7\) \(Ra\) \(\wedge\)E: \(2\)
\(8\) \(Qa \wedge Ra\) \(\wedge\)I: \(6, 7\)
\(9\) \(Qa \wedge Ra\) \(\exists\)E: \(1, 8\qquad\color{red}\textrm{No!}\)

This application of the rule is forbidden by the fact that the constant \(a\) that we introduced as a new name to bear witness to the existential appears in the conclusion of the subproof, which we then infer on the basis of the existential alone. This should obviously not be allowed or else we could infer from the existential claim \(\exists x(\varphi)\) that \(\varphi(a)\), for some specific \(a\).

Another misapplication of the rule involves introducing a witness to the existential that is not a new name but is a constant about which something has already been said. For example:

\[ Pa\rightarrow Qa, \exists x(Px \wedge Rx)\vdash\exists x(Qx\wedge Rx) \]

\(1\) \(\exists x(Px\wedge Rx)\) Premise
\(2\) \(Pa \wedge Ra\) Hypothesis
\(3\) \(Pa \rightarrow Qa\) Premise
\(4\) \(Pa\) \(\wedge\)E: \(3\)
\(5\) \(Qa\) \(\rightarrow\)E: \(3, 4\)
\(6\) \(Ra\) \(\wedge\)E: \(3\)
\(7\) \(Qa \wedge Ra\) \(\wedge\)I: \(5, 6\)
\(8\) \(\exists x(Qx \wedge Rx)\) \(\exists\)I: \(7\)
\(9\) \(\exists x(Qx\wedge Rx)\) \(\exists\)E: \(1, 8\qquad\color{red}\textrm{No!}\)

Here the problem is with the hypothesis in line 2. The name \(a\) which witnesses the existential claim in line 1, is not a new name, for it appears in the premise \(Pa\rightarrow Qa\). So \(a\) is a specific object that we have already said something about, namely, that it is a \(Q\) only if it is a \(P\). We cannot then treat this specific object as an unspecified, or indefinite, witness to the existential, and so we cannot apply existential elimination to line 8.

3  Quantificational Logic

3.1 Introduction

In this course, we have studied two distinct systems of logic: syllogistic logic and propositional logic. The first allowed us to recognize as valid such arguments as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Every Chicago winter is cold\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm}Some Chicago winters are not snowy}}{\textrm{Not all seasons that are cold are snowy}} \]

Propositional logic, on the other hand, taught us how to recognize the validity of arguments such as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{The cat is either on the bed or under the bed\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} The cat is not on the bed}}{\textrm{The cat must be under the bed}} \]

What about the following argument?

\[ \frac{\textrm{Everyone in the classroom is either in first or second grade\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Amy is in the classroom and she is not in first grade}}{\textrm{Amy is in second grade}} \]

While this seems like a valid argument, its validity turns not only on the significance of such syllogistic constructions as ‘every \(\ldots\) is —’, but also on the meaning of propositional operators, like ‘either \(\ldots\) or —’ and ‘not \(\ldots\)’. Thus, to formulate standards for argumentative validity that can apply to such arguments, we need a single system of logic that can comprehend both syllogistic and propositional modes of reasoning.

In this chapter, we will introduce such a system of logic. We will call this system ‘quantificational’ logic.1 In addition to mixed syllogistic-propositional reasoning, the language of quantificational logic will also extend syllogistic logic in another direction. In addition to allowing us to ascribe properties to individual objects, it will allow us to express relations that obtain between multiple objects, so that we can recognize as valid such arguments as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Anyone can go on the trip with a parent's approval\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Adam is Julia's parent}}{\textrm{If Adam gives Julia approval, she can go on the trip}} \]

This capacity for relational reasoning makes quantificational logic very powerful. It so powerful, in fact, that, with only minor emendations, it is capable of formalizing nearly every mathematical proof or deductive argument that you are ever likely to encounter.

3.2 The Syntax of Quantificational Logic

Before introducing the formal language of quantificational logic, let’s begin by considering a few specific arguments. By formalizing these arguments, we will get a rough sense for the various kinds of syntactic items that we will need to include in our language. Consider the argument:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Everyone in the classroom is either in first or second grade\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Amy is in the classroom and she is not in first grade}}{\textrm{Amy is in second grade}} \]

Take the second premise:

\[ \textrm{Amy is in the classroom and she is not in first grade} \]

This looks like a propositional compound, more specifically, it looks like a conjunction, and even more specifically a conjunction with a negated right conjunct. Thus, in the language of propositional logic, we might have formalized this sentence as follows:

\[C\wedge\neg F\]

where

\(C\): Amy is in the classroom

\(F\): Amy is in first grade

To express such propositional compounds, our formal language will still need to include all the propositional connectives that we introduced in the context of propositional logic, namely:

\[ \neg, \wedge, \vee, \rightarrow, \leftrightarrow \]

The above formalization in the language of propositional logic, is probably not going to suffice. To see why, consider the conclusion of the argument:

\[\textrm{Amy is in second grade}\]

How should we formalize this sentence? Since it is not a propositional compound, in the language of propositional logic it would simply be formalized by another atomic sentence, such as \(S\). But representing the second premise as \(C\wedge\neg F\) and the conclusion as \(S\) obscures the crucial relationship that obtains between the two, namely, that they are both sentences which say something about the same person, Amy. So both sentences must somehow refer to Amy. Now, if we want to refer to Amy in our formal language, we will need to give her a name. So, our formal language will need to have names of individuals. Let’s use the letter ‘\(a\)’ as a name for Amy.

The second premise says two things about Amy. The first is that she is in the classroom and the second is that she is not in the first grade. When we say that Amy is in the classroom, we are ascribing to Amy a specific property. So, our language will need to include not only names for objects, but names for properties as well. Let \(C\) be a name for the property ‘… is in the classroom’. We want to assert that this property holds of Amy. To attribute this property to Amy, we will first write down a name of the property, followed by a name of Amy. So, all told, the claim that Amy is in the classroom will be written:

\[ Ca \]

Let \(F\) name the property ‘… is in the first grade’. Then the second premise can be formalized as follows:

\[ Ca\wedge \neg Fa\]

While this rendering conveys the compound propositional structure just as clearly as the formalization of the sentence in propositional logic, it adds internal structure to the components sentences out of which this compound is built. Instead of treating these component sentences as structureless, or atomic, we have now indicated something about these sentences, namely, that they attribute properties to a specific individual, an individual who is referred to by name and who can be referred to again by that same name elsewhere in the argument. For example, letting \(S\) be the property ‘… is in the second grade’, the conclusion of the argument can now be rendered: \[ Sa \]

Now, let’s look at the first premise of the argument:

\[ \textrm{Everyone in the classroom is either in first or second grade} \]

In the language of syllogistic logic, we might have formalized this sentence as follows:

\[C\textrm{\textbf{a}}E\]

where the term \(C\) denotes the property ‘… is in the classroom’ and \(E\) the property ‘… is either in first or second grade’. This sort of rendering is also unlikely to be adequate because it doesn’t expose the disjunctive structure of the predicate term, which plays an essential role in the reasoning.

Now, this sentence clearly says something about individuals, but it does so without mentioning anyone by name. Since the sentence doesn’t name anyone, let’s just imagine some arbitrary individual, and call this arbitrary individual \(x\). What does the sentence imply about \(x\)? It implies that if \(x\) is in the classroom, then \(x\) is either in first or second grade. Using the same notation introduced in our above formalization, we can express this fact about \(x\) as follows:

\[ Cx \rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx) \]

Now what the premise says is that the above claim is true not just of some particular individual, but of every individual. To convey this fact, we need a way of indicating that the claim is meant to be true regardless of who \(x\) stands for. We could indicate this as follows:

\[ \textrm{For all $x$: } Cx \rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx) \]

In the language of quantificational logic, in place of the prefix ‘For all \(x\) …:’ we write \(\forall x\). The upside-down ‘A’ written before the name \(x\) is called a quantifier, and more specifically the universal quantifier. All told then, the first premise can be written:

\[\forall x(Cx\rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx))\]

To express this sentence, we needed to introduce two new elements into the language: the universal quantifier and the symbol \(x\). We will call the symbol \(x\) a variable. Syntactically, it will function very much like a name, but its reference will be allowed to vary.

The entire argument, formalized in the language of quantificational logic is:

\[ \forall x(Cx\rightarrow (Fx \vee Sx)),Ca\wedge \neg Fa\vdash Sa \]

We will later see how to assess the validity of this argument.

Now, let’s examine the second argument mentioned above:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Anyone can go on the trip with a parent's approval\hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} Adam is Julia's parent}}{\textrm{If Adam gives Julia approval, she can go on the trip}} \]

Consider the second premise:

\[ \textrm{Adam is Julia's parent} \]

This sentence says something not just about one individual, but about two, namely Adam and Julia. What does it say about them? It says that they stand in a certain relation to each other, in particular, that Adam is a parent of Julia. To capture this fact we will need to give a name to the two-place relation ‘… is a parent of —’. Let’s call it \(P\). To express the fact that Adam stands in this relation to Julia, we will first write down the name of the relation, then the names of Adam and Julia in that order. If ‘a’ names Adam and ‘j’ names Julia, then we can formalize the sentence as follows:

\[ Paj \]

Note here that the order in which the names appears matters. If we had instead written \(Pja\) this would mean that Julia is a parent of Adam. This, of course, is not the case since the relation of parent to child is asymmetrical. Why not write \(aRj\) to make the order a bit more perspicuous? We could, but our logic will later allow for relations between three or more things, and the prefix (as opposed to the infix) notation can be more straightforwardly adapted to handle these more complex relations.

So now we see that, in addition to names for properties, we will also need to include in our language names for relations. Syntactically, names for relations will function very much like names for properties, only instead of applying to a single individual they will apply to two (or three, or possibly more) individuals. We will call both names for properties and names for relations, predicates. Predicates may be unary if they denote properties of individuals, or they may be binary if they denote two-place relations, or ternary if they denote three-place relations, or quaternary etc..

Now let’s look at the conclusion. Clearly, this is a conditional sentence. The antecedent of the conditional is ‘Adam gives Julia approval’. Again, this states that a certain relation obtains between Adam and Julia, namely, the relation ‘… gives approval to —’. Let’s name this two-place relation by the binary predicate \(A\). The consequent of the conditional is the sentence ‘Julia can go on the trip’. Here we are simply ascribing a property to Julia. Let \(T\) be the unary predicate that names the property ‘… can go on the trip’. Then the conclusion of the argument can be written: \[ Aaj\rightarrow Tj\]

Now for the first premise. This says something about individuals without naming any individual explicitly. If \(x\) and \(y\) are two arbitrary individuals, then the sentence implies that \(x\) can go on the trip, provided \(x\) gets the approval of \(y\) and \(y\) is a parent of \(x\). Let’s focus on the proviso first, which states that \(x\) must have a parent’s approval. We could write down this proviso as follows: \[Pyx \wedge Ayx\] Note that the proviso does not state that \(x\) needs the approval of all of their parents, just that \(x\) needs the approval of some one of them. The first stronger proviso, requiring approval from all parents, could be written: \[\forall y(Pyx \wedge Ayx)\] But what we need instead is a way to say something like \[\textrm{For some $y$:} Pyx \wedge Ayx\] To say this, we will introduce a second quantifier, which we will call the existential quantifier and which we will denote by the symbol, \(\exists\). The proviso can thus be written:

\[\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\]

This says that \(x\) gets approval from some parent. What we want to say about \(x\) is that if \(x\) gets parent approval, then \(x\) can go on the trip:

\[\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow T(x)\]

What the sentence says is that this is true not just of some \(x\), but of every \(x\). So, all told the premise can be written:

\[ \forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow T(x)) \]

The argument can thus be formalized:

\[ \forall x(\exists y(Pyx\wedge Ayx)\rightarrow T(x)), Paj\vdash Aaj\rightarrow Tj \]

Now that we have a sense of the types of syntactic item needed to formalize arguments in quantificational logic, we can give a more precise specification of the language. We will call the language L\(_Q\). As usual, we begin by supplying an alphabet for the language.

The alphabet for L\(_Q\) includes an arbitrarily large stock of symbols that we will refer to as constants. The constant symbols will serve as our names for individual objects. In addition to constants, the language also includes an arbitrarily large stock of symbols that we will refer to as variables. Collectively, the constants and variables will be referred to as terms. We could, of course, use whatever symbols we like for the terms of the language, but we will generally adopt the convention of using lowercase letters from the beginning or middle of the Latin alphabet for constants, and lowercase letters from the end of the Latin alphabet for variables.

  • Terms:

    • Constants: \(a,b,c,...j,k,l,...\)

    • Variables: \(x,y,z,u,v,w,...\)

In addition to the terms, we will also need predicate symbols that can serve as names for properties and relations. Again, any symbols will do, but, by convention, we will typically use uppercase Latin letters for predicate symbols.

  • Predicates: \(A,B,C,\ldots,P,Q,R,\ldots\)

We will assume that every predicate in the language has associated with it a predetermined number, called its arity, which indicates how many objects are related by the property or relation it signifies. If the arity of a predicate is \(2\), say, then it is a binary predicate and names a two-place relation. If it is \(1\), then it is a unary predicate and names a property. When we wish to indicate that the predicate symbol \(P\) has arity \(n\), we will denote it by \(P^n\).

The language of quantificational logic also include the five propositional connectives:

  • Propositional Connectives: \(\neg, \wedge, \vee, \rightarrow, \leftrightarrow\)

and parentheses that can be used for delimiting their scope:

  • Parentheses: \((\,,\,)\)

Finally, the language also include two additional symbols representing the universal and existential quantifiers:

  • Quantifiers: \(\forall, \exists\)

That’s it. If you go back to the arguments that we just formalized, you can verify for yourself that every symbol appearing in every sentence in these arguments falls into one of these five categories.

Next, we need to say which expressions–that is, which strings of symbols from the alphabet–constitute sentences of L\(_Q\). First we will define the notion of a formula of L\(_Q\).

Definition 3.1 The formulas of L\(_Q\) are defined inductively as follows:

  1. If \(P^n\) is a predicate and \(a_1,\ldots,a_n\) are terms, then: \[P a_1a_2\cdots a_n\] is a formula.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a formula, then \(\neg\varphi\) is a formula.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are formulas and \(\ast\) is a binary propositional connective, then \(\varphi\ast\psi\) is a formula.

  4. If \(\varphi\) is a formula and \(v\) is a variable, then \(\forall v(\varphi)\) is a formula.

  5. If \(\varphi\) is a formula and \(v\) is a variable, then \(\exists v(\varphi)\) is a formula.

Assuming that \(P\) is a unary predicate, \(R\) is a binary predicate, and \(S\) is a ternary predicate, then the following expressions are all formulas of L\(_Q\):

\[ Pa \]

\[ Sxba\]

\[ Rax\leftrightarrow \neg Rbx\]

\[\forall x(Rxa\rightarrow Px)\]

\[\neg\exists y (Pz \wedge \forall z(Rxy\vee Pz))\]

\[\exists x(\forall y((Pa \wedge Px)\rightarrow Rxy))\]

Notice that, in some of these formulas, there are variables that do not occur in the scope of a corresponding quantifier. For example, \(Rax\leftrightarrow \neg Rbx\). In this formula \(x\) occurs, but it does not fall within the scope of either \(\forall x\) or \(\exists x\). In this case, we say that \(x\) occurs freely in the formula. Contrast this with the next formula:

\[\forall x(Rxa\rightarrow Px)\]

Ignoring the \(x\) attached to the quantifier, the other two occurrences of the variable \(x\) both occur within the (parenthetically delimited) scope of the quantifier \(\forall x\). In this case, we say that these occurrences of \(x\) are not free, but bound. Now, consider the formula:

\[\neg\exists y (Pz \wedge \forall z(Rxy\vee Pz))\]

Ignoring the variables attached to quantifiers, which variables in the formula are free and which bound? The first occurrence of the variable \(z\) is free, as is the only occurence of the variable \(x\), but the only occurrence of the variable \(y\) and the second occurrence of the variable \(z\) are bound.

We define a sentence of the language to be any formula in which no variable occurs freely.

Definition 3.2 A formula of L\(_Q\) is a sentence if every variable that occurs in it is bound.

Only sentences of the language can serve as the premises or conclusion of an argument, but non-sentential formulas are important as well. For these formulas effectively allow us to introduce into the language new properties and relations not names by the predicates of the language. Recall our attempt to formalize the claim:

\[ \textrm{Anyone can go on the trip if they are given a parent's approval}\]

To capture the relevant proviso, we began by writing down the following formula:

\[ Pyx \wedge Ayx \]

This is a formula but not a sentence since the variables \(x\) and \(y\) do not occur within the scope of any quantifiers. Because \(x\) and \(y\) occur freely in this formula, the formula in effect says something about the items to which they refer. What does it say? It says that \(x\) and \(y\) stand in a certain relation to one another, namely, the relation of \(y\) being a parent of \(x\) who gives to \(x\) their approval. Notice that this relation is not named by any of the primitive predicates that we used in our formalization of the argument. Rather, it may be thought of as a newly defined binary relation (we will discuss how to apply this relation later).

To capture the proviso, we then said that \(x\) satisfies the required condition if there is some person who stands in this defined relation to \(x\). To express this fact, we applied the existential quantifier to the above formula, resulting in the formula:

\[ \exists y(Pyx \wedge Ayx) \]

Notice that the variable \(y\) no longer occurs freely in the formula since it has been captured, or bound, by the quantifier \(\exists y\). Still this formula is not a sentence, since both occurrences of the variable \(x\) still occur freely in it. Thus, the formula still says something about \(x\). What does it say? It says that \(x\) has a certain property, namely the property of having some parent’s approval. This again can be thought of as a defined property, since the language as specified does not include a unary predicate that names this property directly. To see that this property no longer says anything about \(y\), note that we could define the same property by replacing every occurrence with \(y\) with a different variable, say \(z\):

\[ \exists z(Pzx \wedge Azx) \]

What does this formula say about \(x\)? The same thing as it did before the variable replacement, namely, that \(x\) has a parent who has given them approval. Thus bound variables don’t really name anything, they are, so to speak, ‘dummy’ names. Like pronouns, they are useful, to ensure consistency of reference across a discursive context, as when we say:

\[ \textrm{There is something on the table, and \emph{it} wasn't there before.} \]

Now that we have defined the property of an individual having parental approval, the first premise in the argument is easy to formalize. It says that anyone who has that property can go on the trip.

\[ \forall x(\exists z(Pzx \wedge Azx)\rightarrow T(x)) \]

We have at last arrived at a sentence. All the variables are now bound, and so they are just dummy names. As we shall see in the next section, when we introduce the semantics for quantificational logic, it will turn out that the truth value of a sentence does not depend on which individuals the variables name.

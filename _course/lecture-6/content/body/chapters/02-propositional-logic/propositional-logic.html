2  Propositional Logic

2.1 Introduction

Propositional logic is the study of the inferential relations that obtain between compound propositions, such as:

\[ \textrm{Alice is a philosophy major \emph{and} Bob is majoring in economics.} \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{Either} the train is late \emph{or} the schedule that is posted online is wrong}. \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{If} the battery is not dead, \emph{then} the computer will turn on.} \]

\[ \textrm{You are eligible to vote \emph{if and only if} you are a resident of the county}. \]

Such compound propositions are called ‘truth-functional’ compounds, or, ‘Boolean’ compounds, for short.1 Truth-functional propositional logic forms the core of most other systems of logic utilized in philosophical analysis, including first-order quantificational logic and modal logic.2 It also provides the standard context in which the theory of probability is developed. It thus constitutes the first essential entry in a philosophy student’s logical toolkit. In this chapter, we will introduce a formal language in which to represent arguments of propositional logic, a semantic framework in which to assess their validity, and a proof system in which to carry out propositional reasoning.

2.2 The Syntax of Propositional Logic

2.2.1 Truth-Functional Operations

In natural language, many of the sentences that we assert have a compositional structure. They are, that is, built up from other sentences through the use of certain compound sentence forming operations. Let’s take a simple example. Consider the sentence:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain.} \]

From this sentence, we can construct a new sentence by adding to the verb the negating particle not:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will \emph{not} rain.} \]

Moreover, we can perform the very same operation on other sentences. For example, by negating the verb in the sentence:

\[ \textrm{Alice is a philosophy major.} \]

we obtain a new sentence:

\[ \textrm{Alice is \emph{not} a philosophy major.} \]

It is thus natural to think of the act of negating the verb as a general operation which takes us from one sentence to another, the latter being a compound sentence that is, in some way, made up of the former. We can also imagine compound sentence forming operations which act on more than one sentence. For example, given the two sentences:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain.} \]

and

\[ \textrm{Saturday will be sunny. } \]

we can conjoin these sentences using the coordinating particle and to yield the new sentence:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain \emph{and} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

Here we have an operation which takes two sentences and returns a new compound sentence of which both of the original sentences are a part. Since it takes two sentences, we might call such an operation a binary operation, whereas negation, since it acts on only one sentence, would be unary.

How exactly is the sentence ‘Tomorrow it will not rain’, that results from performing the unary operation of negating the verb, related to the sentence ‘Tomorrow it will rain’ on which this operation is performed. The answer should be clear: the sentence which results from performing the operation of negation is one which is true just in case the sentence on which the operation was performed is false. This is likewise the case, for the sentence ‘Alice is not a philosophy major’ obtained by negating the sentence ‘Alice is a philosophy major’. So, we can think of negation as a general operation that takes a sentence to another sentence which is true just in case the original sentence is false.

What about the conjunction of two sentences? The conjunction of two sentences via the the coordinating particle and yields a sentence which is true just in case both of its component sentences are true. For example:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain \emph{and} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

is true just in case tomorrow there is rain and Saturday is sunny. Thus understood, both negation and conjunction, viewed as compound sentence forming operations, have something in common. Both operations result in sentences the truth value of which is entirely determined by the truth values of the component sentences on which these operations are performed. We will call such operations truth-functional operations since the truth or falsehood of their output is a function of the truth and falsehood of their inputs.

There are lots of examples of non-truth-functional compound sentence forming operations in natural language. Take, for example, the result of combining the sentences:

\[ \textrm{The stock market went down.} \]

and

\[ \textrm{There was a snowstorm in Boston.} \]

via the subordinating conjunction ‘because’ to yield the sentence:

\[ \textrm{The stock market went down \emph{because} there was a snowstorm in Boston.} \]

The truth or falsehood of this claim cannot be determined by that of the component sentences alone. In particular, if both sentences are true, the claim could be either true or false depending on whether the sentences stand in a suitable causal relationship to one another. Here are a few more examples of non-truth-functional compounds:

\[ \textrm{\emph{It could have been the case that} life developed on Mars. } \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{Lois Lane believes that} Superman is Clark Kent.} \]

\[ \textrm{Caesar crossed the Rubicon \emph{after} he was declared emperor of Rome.} \]

In all of these cases, the truth value of the compound sentence is not determined by the truth values of its component parts.

In this chapter, we will be concerned to study the logic of truth-functional operations, like negation and conjunction. Other examples of truth-functional compounds include:

\[ \textrm{\emph{Either} the train is late \emph{or} the schedule that is posted online is wrong}. \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{If} the battery is not dead, \emph{then} the computer will turn on.} \]

\[ \textrm{You are eligible to vote \emph{if and only if} you are a resident of the county}. \]

Our aim will be to develop tools to assess the validity arguments involving such compound sentences—arguments such as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Either James is sick at home or the store is closed \hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} The store is not closed}}{\textrm{James is sick at home}} \]

Just as before, we will study this logic in the context of a simplified formal language. This will allow us to avoid the unnecessary complexity of having to deal with natural language constructions. In English, for example, there are many ways to express the truth-functional operation of conjuntion, such as:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain \emph{and} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain, \emph{but} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{While} tomorrow it will rain, Saturday will be sunny.} \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{Even though} Tomorrow it will rain, Saturday will be sunny.} \]

Moreover, in natural language, sentence forming operators that typically effect truth-functional operations do not always work in a uniform way. For instance, not every act of negating the verb has the effect of producing a sentence that is true just in case the original sentence is false. For example:

\[ \textrm{Thomas is not in a good mood.} \]

does not appear to be true just in case:

\[ \textrm{Thomas is in a good mood.} \]

is false. Both could perhaps be false, if Thomas happens to be in a state of resigned indifference.

To avoid such complications, we will formulate arguments of propositional logic in an artificial language devised for the express purpose of modeling the sort of arguments we are concerned to assess. We will call this formal language L\(_P\).

2.2.2 The Formal Language L\(_P\)

To define the language L\(_P\), we begin by defining an alphabet for the language. This alphabet consists, first, of an arbitrarily large collection of symbols:

\[ P, Q, R, \ldots \]

referred to as atoms. In addition to the atoms, the alphabet also includes five special symbols:

\[ \neg\hspace{0.05in},\hspace{0.05in} \wedge\hspace{0.05in},\hspace{0.05in} \vee\hspace{0.05in},\hspace{0.05in} \rightarrow,\hspace{0.05in} \leftrightarrow \]

These five symbols are referred to collectively as propositional connectives, and, respectively, as the negation operator (\(\neg\)), the conjunction operator (\(\wedge\)), the disjunction operator (\(\vee\)), and the conditional operator (\(\rightarrow\)). The latter three connectives (i.e., \(\wedge, \vee\), and \(\rightarrow\)) are referred to as binary connectives, whereas the negation operator is referred to as a unary connective.

The intended meaning of the connectives is given by the following table:

The intended meanings of the propositional connectives
Connective Operation English Operator
\(\neg\) negation It is not the case that \(\,\underline{\hspace{0.5cm}}\,\).
\(\wedge\) conjunction \(\cdots\) and \(\,\underline{\hspace{0.5cm}}\,\).
\(\vee\) disjunction \(\cdots\) or \(\,\underline{\hspace{0.5cm}}\,\) (or both).
\(\rightarrow\) conditional If \(\cdots\), then \(\,\underline{\hspace{0.5cm}}\,\).
\(\leftrightarrow\) biconditional \(\cdots\) if and only if \(\,\underline{\hspace{0.5cm}}\,\).

Lastly, in addition to the atoms and connectives, the alphabet for L\(_P\) also includes the left and right parentheses:

\[ (\hspace{0.07in},\hspace{0.07in}) \]

As before, we will call an expression of the language any finite string of symbols taken from its alphabet. The sentences of the language will be constructed through the recursive application of the propositional connectives to the atoms. We will define the sentences in the following, somewhat akward way:

Definition 2.1 A sentences of L\(_P\) is any expression that can be judged to be a sentence by appeal to the following facts:

  1. Any expression consisting of a single atom is a sentence.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a sentence, then the expression \(\neg \varphi\) is a sentence.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences and \(\ast\) is a binary connective, then the expression \((\varphi\ast\psi)\) is a sentence.

This sort of definition is what is called in mathematics and logic an inductive definition. We will say more about how inductive definitions work in the next section. For now, we simply note that the following are all examples of sentences of \(L_{\mathbf{P}}\):

\[P\]

\[\neg Q\]

\[(R \leftrightarrow S)\]

\[\neg \neg (P \wedge Q)\]

\[((P\leftrightarrow\neg Q) \wedge(Q\vee R))\]

\[((Q\rightarrow(R\wedge(S \rightarrow P))) \leftrightarrow(P\rightarrow\neg Q))\]

Note that, unlike the sentences of L\(_S\), the sentences of the formal language L\(_P\) can grow to be arbitrarily complex.

We refer to those sentences consisting of a single atom as atomic sentences. The sentence \(\neg \varphi\) is referred to as the negation of \(\varphi\). The sentence \((\varphi\wedge\psi)\) is referred to as the conjunction with left conjunct \(\varphi\) and right conjunct \(\psi\), or simply the conjunction of \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\). The sentence \((\varphi\vee\psi)\) is referred to as the disjunction with left disjunct \(\varphi\) and right disjunct \(\psi\), or simply the disjunction of \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\). The sentence \((\varphi\rightarrow\psi)\) is referred to as the conditional with antecedent \(\varphi\) and consequent \(\psi\). The sentence \((\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi)\) is referred to as the biconditional with left term \(\varphi\) and right term \(\psi\). Conjunctions, disjunctions, conditionals, and biconditionals are referred to, collectively, as binary compounds.

2.2.3 Inductive Definitions

Let’s go back and look more closely at our definition of a sentence of L\(_P\):

Definition 2.2 A sentences of L\(_P\) is any expression that can be judged to be a sentence by appeal to the following facts:

  1. Any expression consisting of a single atom is a sentence.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a sentence, then the expression \(\neg \varphi\) is a sentence.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences and \(\ast\) is a binary connective, then the expression \((\varphi\ast\psi)\) is a sentence.

Consider the expression:

\[ \neg (P \wedge Q)\]

Is this expression a sentence, according to the above definition? The definition says that the expression is a sentence provided it can be judged to be so on the bases of clauses 1–3. Let’s first look at clause 2. This clause states that if \(\varphi\) is a sentence, then \(\neg\varphi\) is a sentences as well. So, in the case of the expression \(\neg(P\wedge Q)\), it suffices to show that this is a sentence to show that \((P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence. How can we confirm that this is true? Have a look at clause 3. This clause says that if \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences, then \((\varphi\ast\psi)\) is also a sentence, where \(\ast\) is any binary connective. It follows from this that the expression \((P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence if both \(P\) and \(Q\) are sentences. How can we confirm that this is true? Look at clause 1. This clause says that any expression consisting of a single atom is a sentence, so since \(P\) and \(Q\) are both expressions of this sort, they are both sentences.

So, it turns out, \(\neg(P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence of L\(_P\). How do we know this? Because \(P\) and \(Q\) are both sentences (by clause 1), and so \((P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence (by clause 3), and so \(\neg(P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence (by clause 2).

A definition that works like this is called an inductive definition. Inductive definitions are very common in mathematics, logic, computer science, and linguistics—any field in which the items to be studied exhibit a recursive compositional structure, like the sentences of a language. Inductive definitions take the following form: they first tell us, flatly, that certain items fall under the defined term. They then make one or more claims to the effect that if certain items fall under that term, then certain other items fall under that term as well. In this way, inductive definitions specify a kind of repeatable, or recursive, procedure by which new items falling under the defined term can be generated or constructed from older ones, and they stipulate that an object belongs to defined class of itms just in case it can be constructed in this way. The clause specifying the starting items from which the construction can proceed is called the base clause of the inductive definition, the rest of the clauses that tell us how to construct new items from old ones, are called the inductive clauses. So, in the above definition of L\(_P\), clause 1 is the base clause and clauses 2 and 3 the inductive ones.

Let’s consider another example of an inductive definition. Suppose we want to give an inductive definition of the natural numbers, that is, the numbers belonging to the set:

\[\{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\]

One way of thinking about how a number gets into this set is as follows: First, we stipulate that \(1\) is in the set. Then, we posit that if a number, say \(n\), is already in the set, then \(n+1\) goes into the set as well. We can turn this idea into an inductive definition as follows:

Definition 2.3 A natural number is any number that can be judged to be a natural number by appeal to the following facts:

  1. \(1\) is a natural number.

  2. If \(n\) is a natural number, then \(n+1\) is a natural number.

It is easy to see that a number like \(17\) is a natural number according to this definition. For, \(1\) is a natural number (by clause 1), and so \(2\) is a natural number (by clause 2), and so \(3\) is a natural number (by clause 2), and so … \(17\) is a natural number (by clause 2).

Note that a single term or notion may have several different inductive definitions. For example, here are two more inductive definitions of the notion of a natural number. The first is fairly obvious, the second less so:

Definition 2.4 A natural number is any number that can be judged to be a natural number by appeal to the following facts:

  1. \(1\) is a natural number.

  2. If \(1, 2, \ldots, n\) are all natural numbers, then \(n+1\) is a natural number.

Definition 2.5 A natural number is any number that can be judged to be a natural number by appeal to the following facts:

  1. Every number that is either \(1\) or a prime is a natural number.

  2. If \(n\) and \(m\) are natural numbers, then \(n\times m\) is a natural number.

One of the main advantages of inductive definitions is that such definitions can provide a straightforward means of proving that a certain property is possessed by every item belonging to the defined class. In particular, if a item is defined inductively, then it suffices to show that:

  1. Every item mentioned in the base clause of the definition has the property in question.

  2. The property is preserved under each of the inductive clauses of the definition. In other words, if all items to which the inductive clause is applied possess the property in question, then any new items which fall under the term in accordance with this clause have that property as well.

A proof that proceeds in this way is called a proof by induction, or an inductive proof—not to be confused with an inductive argument in the sense of David Hume! Here is an example of an inductive proof, using the above definition of a natural number.

Theorem 2.1 For any natural number \(n\):

\[ 1+2+\cdots+n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \]

Proof. We first observe that the claim holds for \(n=1\):

\[ 1 = \frac{1(2)}{2} \]

Now, suppose that the claim holds for the natural number \(n\). Then:

\[ \begin{align} 1 + 2 + \cdots + n + (n + 1) & = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2} + (n + 1)\quad \\ & = \frac{n(n + 1) + 2(n + 1)}{2}\: \\ & = \frac{(n + 1)(n + 2)}{2} \end{align} \]

So the claim also holds for \(n+1\). This completes the proof.

While an inductive proof such as this may seem like magic, much of the challenge of supplying inductive proofs consists in identifying suitable inductive definitions for the defined class of items.

One useful way of conceptualizing the items picked out by an inductive definition is by means of a ‘formation tree’. This is a tree structure, each node of which is labeled by an item falling under the defined term. The direct descendants of any given node in the tree are items which generate their parent node in accordance with one of inductive clauses of the definition, and the root nodes of the tree (the nodes with no direct descendants) are items falling under the term in accordance with the base clause of the definition.

  1. The label ‘Boolean’ is in honor of the English mathematician George Boole, whose seminal work, An Investigation of the Laws of Thought (1854), layed the foundation for the branch of mathematics now called Boolean algebra.↩︎

  2. Truth-functional, or Boolean, propositional logic is often referred to as classical propositional logic. This label is a misnomer, for truth-functional propositional logic is neither classical in the sense of being the first system of propositional logic to have been studied, nor in the sense of having its origin in Greek or Latin antiquity. The use of the label ‘classical’ to describe truth-functional propositional logic seems to have gained widespread usage in the mid-20th century, mainly to distinguish truth-functional propositional logic from alternative non-classical systems of propositional logic (such as intuitionistic propositional logic or relevantist propositional logic) that began to be studied more widely at that time.↩︎

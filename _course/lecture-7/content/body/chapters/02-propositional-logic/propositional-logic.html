2  Propositional Logic

2.1 Introduction

Propositional logic is the study of the inferential relations that obtain between compound propositions, such as:

\[ \textrm{Alice is a philosophy major \emph{and} Bob is majoring in economics.} \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{Either} the train is late \emph{or} the schedule that is posted online is wrong}. \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{If} the battery is not dead, \emph{then} the computer will turn on.} \]

\[ \textrm{You are eligible to vote \emph{if and only if} you are a resident of the county}. \]

Propositional logic forms the core of most other systems of logic utilized in philosophical analysis, including first-order quantificational logic and modal logic. It also provides the standard context in which the theory of probability is developed. It thus constitutes the first essential entry in a philosophy student’s logical toolkit. In this chapter, we will introduce a formal language in which to represent arguments of propositional logic, a semantic framework in which to assess their validity, and a proof system in which to carry out propositional reasoning.

2.2 The Syntax of Propositional Logic

2.2.1 Truth-Functional Operations

In natural language, many of the sentences that we assert have a compositional structure. They are, that is, built up from other sentences through the use of certain compound sentence forming operations. Let’s take a simple example. Consider the sentence:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain.} \]

From this sentence, we can construct a new sentence by adding to the verb the negating particle not:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will \emph{not} rain.} \]

Moreover, we can perform the very same operation on other sentences. For example, by negating the verb in the sentence:

\[ \textrm{Alice is a philosophy major.} \]

we obtain a new sentence:

\[ \textrm{Alice is \emph{not} a philosophy major.} \]

It is thus natural to think of the act of negating the verb as a general operation which takes us from one sentence to another, the latter being a compound sentence that is, in some way, made up of the former. We can also imagine compound sentence forming operations which act on more than one sentence. For example, given the two sentences:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain.} \]

and

\[ \textrm{Saturday will be sunny. } \]

we can conjoin these sentences using the coordinating particle and to yield the new sentence:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain \emph{and} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

Here we have an operation which takes two sentences and returns a new compound sentence of which both of the original sentences are a part. Since it takes two sentences, we might call such an operation a binary operation, whereas negation, since it acts on only one sentence, would be unary.

How exactly is the sentence ‘Tomorrow it will not rain’, that results from performing the unary operation of negating the verb, related to the sentence ‘Tomorrow it will rain’ on which this operation is performed. The answer should be clear: the sentence which results from performing the operation of negation is one which is true just in case the sentence on which the operation was performed is false. This is likewise the case, for the sentence ‘Alice is not a philosophy major’ obtained by negating the sentence ‘Alice is a philosophy major’. So, we can think of negation as a general operation that takes a sentence to another sentence which is true just in case the original sentence is false.

What about the conjunction of two sentences? The conjunction of two sentences via the the coordinating particle and yields a sentence which is true just in case both of its component sentences are true. For example:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain \emph{and} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

is true just in case tomorrow there is rain and Saturday is sunny. Thus understood, both negation and conjunction, viewed as compound sentence forming operations, have something in common. Both operations result in sentences the truth value of which is entirely determined by the truth values of the component sentences on which these operations are performed. Such operations are called truth-functional operations since the truth or falsehood of their output is a function of the truth and falsehood of their inputs.1

There are lots of examples of non-truth-functional compound sentence forming operations in natural language. Take, for example, the result of combining the sentences:

\[ \textrm{The stock market went down.} \]

and

\[ \textrm{There was a snowstorm in Boston.} \]

via the subordinating conjunction ‘because’ to yield the sentence:

\[ \textrm{The stock market went down \emph{because} there was a snowstorm in Boston.} \]

The truth or falsehood of this claim cannot be determined by that of the component sentences alone. In particular, if both sentences are true, the claim could be either true or false depending on whether the sentences stand in a suitable causal relationship to one another. Here are a few more examples of non-truth-functional compounds:

\[ \textrm{\emph{It could have been the case that} life developed on Mars. } \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{Lois Lane believes that} Superman is Clark Kent.} \]

\[ \textrm{Caesar crossed the Rubicon \emph{after} he was declared emperor of Rome.} \]

In all of these cases, the truth value of the compound sentence is not determined by the truth values of its component parts.

In this chapter, we will be concerned to study the logic of truth-functional operations, like negation and conjunction. Other examples of truth-functional compounds include:

\[ \textrm{\emph{Either} the train is late \emph{or} the schedule that is posted online is wrong}. \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{If} the battery is not dead, \emph{then} the computer will turn on.} \]

\[ \textrm{You are eligible to vote \emph{if and only if} you are a resident of the county}. \]

Our aim will be to develop tools to assess the validity of arguments involving such compound sentences—arguments such as:

\[ \frac{\textrm{Either James is sick at home or the store is closed \hspace{3mm},\hspace{3mm} The store is not closed}}{\textrm{James is sick at home}} \]

Just as before, we will study this logic in the context of a simplified formal language. This will allow us to avoid the unnecessary complexity of having to deal with natural language constructions. In English, for example, there are many ways to express the truth-functional operation of conjuntion, such as:

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain \emph{and} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

\[ \textrm{Tomorrow it will rain, \emph{but} Saturday will be sunny.} \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{While} tomorrow it will rain, Saturday will be sunny.} \]

\[ \textrm{\emph{Even though} Tomorrow it will rain, Saturday will be sunny.} \]

Moreover, in natural language, sentence forming operators that typically effect truth-functional operations do not always work in a uniform way. For instance, not every act of negating the verb has the effect of producing a sentence that is true just in case the original sentence is false. For example:

\[ \textrm{Thomas is not in a good mood.} \]

does not appear to be true just in case:

\[ \textrm{Thomas is in a good mood.} \]

is false. Both could perhaps be false, if Thomas happens to be in a state of resigned indifference.

To avoid such complications, we will formulate arguments of propositional logic in an artificial language devised for the express purpose of modeling the sort of arguments we are concerned to assess. We will call this formal language L\(_P\).2

2.2.2 The Formal Language L\(_P\)

To define the language L\(_P\), we begin by defining an alphabet for the language. This alphabet consists, first, of an arbitrarily large collection of symbols:

\[ P, Q, R, \ldots \]

referred to as atoms. In addition to the atoms, the alphabet also includes five special symbols:

\[ \neg\hspace{0.05in},\hspace{0.05in} \wedge\hspace{0.05in},\hspace{0.05in} \vee\hspace{0.05in},\hspace{0.05in} \rightarrow,\hspace{0.05in} \leftrightarrow \]

These five symbols are referred to collectively as propositional connectives, and, respectively, as the negation operator (\(\neg\)), the conjunction operator (\(\wedge\)), the disjunction operator (\(\vee\)), and the conditional operator (\(\rightarrow\)). The latter three connectives (i.e., \(\wedge, \vee\), and \(\rightarrow\)) are referred to as binary connectives, whereas the negation operator is referred to as a unary connective.

As before, we will call an expression of the language any finite string of symbols taken from its alphabet. The sentences of the language will be constructed through the recursive application of the propositional connectives to the atoms. We will define the sentences in the following, somewhat awkward way:

Definition 2.1 A sentences of L\(_P\) is any expression that can be judged to be a sentence by appeal to the following facts:

  1. Any expression consisting of a single atom is a sentence.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a sentence, then the expression \(\neg \varphi\) is a sentence.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences and \(\ast\) is a binary connective, then the expression \((\varphi\ast\psi)\) is a sentence.

This sort of definition is what is called in mathematics and logic an inductive definition. We will say more about how inductive definitions work in the next section. For now, we simply note that the following are all examples of sentences of \(L_{\mathbf{P}}\):

\[P\]

\[\neg Q\]

\[(R \leftrightarrow S)\]

\[\neg \neg (P \wedge Q)\]

\[((P\leftrightarrow\neg Q) \wedge(Q\vee R))\]

\[((Q\rightarrow(R\wedge(S \rightarrow P))) \leftrightarrow(P\rightarrow\neg Q))\]

Note that, unlike the sentences of L\(_S\), the sentences of the formal language L\(_P\) can grow to be arbitrarily complex.

We refer to those sentences consisting of a single atom as atomic sentences. The sentence \(\neg \varphi\) is referred to as the negation of \(\varphi\). The sentence \((\varphi\wedge\psi)\) is referred to as the conjunction with left conjunct \(\varphi\) and right conjunct \(\psi\), or simply the conjunction of \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\). The sentence \((\varphi\vee\psi)\) is referred to as the disjunction with left disjunct \(\varphi\) and right disjunct \(\psi\), or simply the disjunction of \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\). The sentence \((\varphi\rightarrow\psi)\) is referred to as the conditional with antecedent \(\varphi\) and consequent \(\psi\). The sentence \((\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi)\) is referred to as the biconditional with left term \(\varphi\) and right term \(\psi\). Conjunctions, disjunctions, conditionals, and biconditionals are referred to, collectively, as binary compounds.

2.2.3 Inductive Definitions

Let’s go back and look more closely at our definition of a sentence of L\(_P\):

Definition 2.2 A sentences of L\(_P\) is any expression that can be judged to be a sentence by appeal to the following facts:

  1. Any expression consisting of a single atom is a sentence.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a sentence, then the expression \(\neg \varphi\) is a sentence.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences and \(\ast\) is a binary connective, then the expression \((\varphi\ast\psi)\) is a sentence.

Consider the expression:

\[ \neg (P \wedge Q)\]

Is this expression a sentence, according to the above definition? The definition says that the expression is a sentence provided it can be judged to be so on the bases of clauses 1–3. Let’s first look at clause 2. This clause states that if \(\varphi\) is a sentence, then \(\neg\varphi\) is a sentences as well. So, in the case of the expression \(\neg(P\wedge Q)\), it suffices to show that this is a sentence to show that \((P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence. How can we confirm that this is true? Have a look at clause 3. This clause says that if \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences, then \((\varphi\ast\psi)\) is also a sentence, where \(\ast\) is any binary connective. It follows from this that the expression \((P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence if both \(P\) and \(Q\) are sentences. How can we confirm that this is true? Look at clause 1. This clause says that any expression consisting of a single atom is a sentence, so since \(P\) and \(Q\) are both expressions of this sort, they are both sentences.

So, it turns out, \(\neg(P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence of L\(_P\). How do we know this? Because \(P\) and \(Q\) are both sentences (by clause 1), and so \((P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence (by clause 3), and so \(\neg(P\wedge Q)\) is a sentence (by clause 2).

A definition that works like this is called an inductive definition. Inductive definitions are very common in mathematics, logic, computer science, and linguistics—any field in which the items to be studied exhibit a recursive compositional structure, like the sentences of a language. Inductive definitions take the following form: they first tell us, flatly, that certain items fall under the defined term. They then make one or more claims to the effect that if certain items fall under that term, then certain other items fall under that term as well. In this way, inductive definitions specify a kind of repeatable, or recursive, procedure by which new items falling under the defined term can be generated or constructed from older ones, and they stipulate that an object belongs to defined class of items just in case it can be constructed in this way. The clause specifying the starting items from which the construction can proceed is called the base clause of the inductive definition, the rest of the clauses that tell us how to construct new items from old ones, are called the inductive clauses. So, in the above definition of L\(_P\), clause 1 is the base clause and clauses 2 and 3 the inductive ones.

Let’s consider another example of an inductive definition. Suppose we want to give an inductive definition of the natural numbers, that is, the numbers belonging to the set:

\[\{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\]

One way of thinking about how a number gets into this set is as follows: first, we stipulate that \(1\) is in the set. Then, we posit that if a number, say \(n\), is already in the set, then \(n+1\) goes into the set as well. We can turn this idea into an inductive definition as follows:

Definition 2.3 A natural number is any number that can be judged to be a natural number by appeal to the following facts:

  1. \(1\) is a natural number.

  2. If \(n\) is a natural number, then \(n+1\) is a natural number.

It is easy to see that a number like \(17\) is a natural number according to this definition. For, \(1\) is a natural number (by clause 1), and so \(2\) is a natural number (by clause 2), and so \(3\) is a natural number (by clause 2), and so … \(17\) is a natural number (by clause 2).

Note that a single term or notion may have several different inductive definitions. For example, here are two more inductive definitions of the notion of a natural number. The first is fairly obvious, the second less so:

Definition 2.4 A natural number is any number that can be judged to be a natural number by appeal to the following facts:

  1. \(1\) is a natural number.

  2. If \(1, 2, \ldots, n\) are all natural numbers, then \(n+1\) is a natural number.

Definition 2.5 A natural number is any number that can be judged to be a natural number by appeal to the following facts:

  1. Every number that is either \(1\) or a prime is a natural number.

  2. If \(n\) and \(m\) are natural numbers, then \(n\times m\) is a natural number.

One of the main advantages of inductive definitions is that such definitions can provide a straightforward means of proving that a certain property is possessed by every item belonging to the defined class. In particular, if an item is defined inductively, then it suffices to show that:

  1. Every item mentioned in the base clause of the definition has the property in question.

  2. The property is preserved under each of the inductive clauses of the definition. In other words, if all items to which the inductive clause is applied possess the property in question, then any new items which fall under the term in accordance with this clause have that property as well.

A proof that proceeds in this way is called a proof by induction, or an inductive proof—not to be confused with an inductive argument in the sense of David Hume! Here is an example of an inductive proof, using the above definition of a natural number.

Theorem 2.1 For any natural number \(n\):

\[ 1+2+\cdots+n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \]

Proof. We first observe that the claim holds for \(n=1\):

\[ 1 = \frac{1(2)}{2} \]

Now, suppose that the claim holds for the natural number \(n\). Then:

\[ \begin{align} 1 + 2 + \cdots + n + (n + 1) & = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2} + (n + 1)\quad \\ & = \frac{n(n + 1) + 2(n + 1)}{2}\: \\ & = \frac{(n + 1)(n + 2)}{2} \end{align} \]

So the claim also holds for \(n+1\). This completes the proof.

While an inductive proof such as this may seem like magic, much of the challenge of supplying inductive proofs consists in identifying suitable inductive definitions for the defined class of items.

2.3 The Meaning of the Propositional Connectives

2.3.1 Truth Tables

Our next task is to explain what makes an argument in the language L\(_P\) a valid argument. Just as before, the idea of argumentative validity will be expressed in terms of necessary truth preservation. An argument is valid just in case the truth of the conclusion follows necessarily from that of the premises. Moreover, the relevant notion of necessity will still be made out in semantic terms. An argument is valid just in case there is no interpretation of the non-logical elements of the language under which the premises are true and the conclusion false.

What are the non-logical elements of the language L\(_P\)? In this case, they are the atoms. These, naturally, are meant to stand for the sort of simple propositions out of which compound propositions are constructed. So, an argument is valid just in case the truth of the conclusion follows from that of the premises, no matter what propositions the atoms stand for.

But what exactly is a proposition? From the perspective of the general philosophy of language, this, of course, is a complicated question. But from the point of view of truth-functional propositional logic, it admits of a fairly straightforward answer. In the context of logic, what is important about an interpretation of a language is that it serves to underwrite a specific truth valuation of the sentences of the language. Since we are dealing exclusively with truth-functional operations, the truth values of compound sentences will be fully determined by the truth values of the component sentences out of which they are formed. In this way, the truth values of all the sentences of the language will supervene on the truth values of the atomic ones.

So, while the atomic sentences do represent simple propositions, the only feature of these propositions that is semantically relevant in the sense that it contributes to assessing the truth values of others sentences in the language, is their own truth or falsehood. Thus, in this context, we may think of a simple proposition as nothing more than the bearer of a truth value, and the possible semantic values of the atoms as the possible truth values of the propositions they express. The only assumptions we will make about these simple propositions are (1) that each is logically contingent in the sense that each is a proposition that may be either true or false; and (2) that they are logically independent of each other in the sense that the truth or falsehood of any one such proposition imposes no constraints on the truth or falsehood of any other.

This means that, we will include among our interpretations of the language all possible assignments of truth values to the atoms. We thus arrive at the following definition:

Definition 2.7 An interpretation of the language L\(_P\) is a function \(\sigma\) which assigns to each of the atoms a truth value, i.e., an object from the set \(\{\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{F}\}\).

To complete our specification of the semantics for the language, we must explain how a given interpretation \(\sigma\) determines a unique truth valuation \(v^\sigma\) for the language. In doing so, we will, in effect, be explaining the meanings of the logical elements of the syntax, that is, the propositional connectives.

For the atomic sentences, the truth value of a sentence is given directly by the interpretation as follows:

(Atomic Sentences): If \(\varphi\) is an atom, then \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\sigma(\varphi)\).3

To assign truth values to compound sentences, we must introduce additional semantic rules, specifying the meaning of the propositional connectives. We do so with the aim of interpreting the propositional connectives as effecting roughly the same semantic operations as the ordinary English locutions to which they correspond in Figure 2.1.

Let’s begin with negation. We have already said that the truth-functional operation effected by negation is that of transforming one sentence into another sentence that is true just in case the original sentence is false. This can be captured by the following rule:

(Negation): For any sentence \(\varphi\): \(v^\sigma(\neg\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\) iff \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{F}\).

Notice the ‘iff’ here. Since we are still assuming that every sentence is either true or false, and that no sentence is both, it follows from our rule for negation that if \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\), then \(v^\sigma(\neg\varphi)=\mathbf{F}\).

Now, let’s consider conjunction. Again, as we’ve already said the truth-functional operation of conjunction takes two sentences and returns a new sentence which is true iff both of its component sentences—or conjuncts—are true. We can describe this operation as follows:

(Conjunction): For any sentences \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\): \(v^\sigma((\varphi\wedge\psi))=\mathbf{T}\) iff \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\) and \(v^\sigma(\psi)=\mathbf{T}\).

With the rules for negation and conjunction in place, we can now consider the truth conditions for more complicated compound sentences, such as \(\neg(P\wedge \neg Q)\). How does the truth value of this sentence depend upon the truth values of the atoms \(P\) and \(Q\)? By the rule for negation, the sentence \(\neg(P\wedge\neg Q)\) is true iff the sentence \((P\wedge\neg Q)\) is false. By the rule for conjunction, \((P\wedge\neg Q)\) is false iff either \(P\) is false or \(\neg Q\) is false (or both). Since \(\neg Q\) is false iff \(Q\) is true, the whole sentence \(\neg(P\wedge\neg Q)\) is true iff either \(P\) is false or \(Q\) is true (or both).

One particularly convenient format for expressing the truth conditions for compound sentences is called a truth table. In a truth table, each of the atoms appearing in the sentence is assigned a separate column and each row corresponds to a different possible assignment of truth values to these atoms. The final column in the table then specifies the truth values of the compound sentence in question for each interpretation of the atoms. For example, a truth table for the sentence \(\neg(P\wedge\neg Q)\) is as follows:

\(P\) \(Q\) \(\neg(P\wedge\neg Q)\)
\(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{F}\)
\(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)

Note that this truth table has four rows. That is because the sentence in question contains two atoms. Since each atom can take one of two truth values, in order to countenance all possible interpretations, we need \(2\times 2 = 4\) rows. If we considered a sentence containing three distinct atoms, we would need a truth table with \(2 \times 2\times 2 = 8\) rows. In general, a sentence with \(n\) distinct atoms requires a truth table with \(2^n\) rows.

When populating the initial columns in a truth table, it is important to ensure that all possible interpretations of the atoms are countenanced. The most straightforward way to ensure that this is the case is to enumerate the truth values of the atoms in accordance with the following systematic procedure: in the first column, fill in the first half of the rows with \(\mathbf{T}\) and the second half with \(\mathbf{F}\). In the second column, fill in the first quarter of the rows with \(\mathbf{T}\), the second quarter with \(\mathbf{F}\), the third quarter with \(\mathbf{T}\), and the last quarter with \(\mathbf{F}\). Continue in this way, writing down alternating blocks of \(\mathbf{T}\) and \(\mathbf{F}\) and at each new column cutting the size of the blocks in half, until you reach the last column for an atom in which \(\mathbf{T}\) and \(\mathbf{F}\) alternate row by row.

2.3.2 Logical Equivalence

Now, let’s return to our specification of the meanings of the propositional connectives. Disjunction corresponds to the English language operation ‘either … or —’. Now, in natural language there exists a certain ambiguity in how we interpret such expressions. Sometimes we interpret these expressions ‘exclusively’ as meaning that one of the two component sentences is true, but not both. For instance:

Either you may have coffee or you may have tea.

In other cases, we interpret disjunctions ‘inclusively’ as meaning that at least one, and possibly both, of the component sentences are true, as in:

Either the train is late or the schedule posted online is wrong.

In specifying the meaning of the disjunction operator in the language L\(_P\), we will assume that it signifies inclusive disjunction, and hence that a disjunction is true just in case either (or both) of its component sentences—or, disjuncts—is true:

(Disjunction): For any sentences \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\): \(v^\sigma((\varphi\wedge\psi))=\mathbf{T}\) iff \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\) or \(v^\sigma(\psi)=\mathbf{T}\) (or both).

The truth table for the sentence \(P\vee Q\) is as follows:

\(P\) \(Q\) \(P\vee Q\)
\(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{F}\)

Exercise 2.4 Let \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) be any sentences of L\(_P\). Write down a sentence of L\(_P\) that expresses the exclusive disjunction of \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\).

We are now in a position to understand why we needed to introduce parentheses into our language of propositional logic. Suppose we omitted the parentheses and allowed for unparenthesized sentences such as \(P\wedge Q\vee R\). As we observed in Section 2.2.3, this sentence would have two distinct formation trees. What the semantic rules for conjunction and disjunction tell us is, in effect, how truth values assigned to the terminal nodes of such a tree, labeled by atoms, are transmitted up through the tree to the compound sentence labeling the root node of the tree, in which those atoms figure. It would, of course, be a bad thing if some assignment of truth values to the atoms, transmits to the root node of one of the formation trees for a sentence a truth value that is different from that which it transmits to the root node of another formation tree for that same sentence. For in this case, the rules would generate an inconsistency, implying that the sentence is both true and false.

It turns out that this sort of incoherency would be possible if we omitted parentheses from the language. For example, let \(\sigma(P)=\mathbf{F}\), \(\sigma(Q)=\mathbf{T}\), and \(\sigma(R)=\mathbf{T}\). Then, transmitting these values up through the two formation trees for the sentence \(P\wedge Q\vee R\) in accordance with the rules for conjunction and disjunction results in different truth values being assigned to this sentence. Following one tree it is assigned the value false:

But following the other it is assigned the value true:

To avoid this sort of semantic incoherence, we need our language to distinguish between these two sentences. This is why we introduced parentheses into our language. By means of parentheses, we enforce a strict ‘order of operations’ which ensures that no sentence admits of multiple distinct formation trees. In this way, we disambiguate the sentence \(P\wedge Q\vee R\), associating its first formation tree with the sentence \((P\wedge(Q\vee R))\) and its second formation tree with the sentence \(((P\wedge Q)\vee R)\).

Now, that we have better understood the need for parentheses, we can be a bit more lax in our use of parentheses for the sake of readability. In particular, when writing sentences, we will omit the outermost pair of parentheses. We will also assume an order of operations for the propositional connectives that will allow us to express sentences using fewer parentheses than are strictly required by the grammatical rules of L\(_P\). In accordance with this order of operations, negations are to be parenthesized first, in as narrow a manner as possible, then conjunctions and disjunctions, then conditionals and, finally, biconditionals. Thus, for example, to express the sentence: \[ ((\neg P\wedge Q)\rightarrow \neg R) \] we may write: \[ \neg P \wedge Q \rightarrow\neg R \]

Exercise 2.5 If we wish to omit the parentheses from our language in a manner which avoids semantic ambiguity, we can adopt what is called a prefix (rather than the standard infix) notation for expressing sentential compounds. This prefix notation is sometimes referred to as Polish notation.

In Polish notation, we define the sentences of the language inductively as follows:

  1. Any expression consisting of a single atom is an sentence.

  2. If \(\varphi\) is a sentence then \(\neg \varphi\) is a sentence.

  3. If \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are sentences and \(*\) is a binary connective, then the expression \(\ast \varphi \psi\) is a sentence.

For example, in Polish notation, the sentence with the same formation tree as \((P\wedge (Q\vee R))\) is written:

\[ \wedge P\!\vee\! Q R \]

while the sentence with the same formation tree as \(((P\wedge Q)\vee R)\) is written:

\[ \vee \!\wedge\! P Q R \]

Let’s now consider the conditional operator. This operator is meant to model the English language construction ‘if …, then —’. But of all the connectives, it is however, perhaps the farthest removed from its natural language counterpart. This is because conditional constructions are used to express a variety of different relationships between sentences, most of which are not truth-functional. For example:

\[ \textrm{If the match is struck, then it will light.} \]

This conditional expresses a causal relationship, and so, as we have already noted, its truth value does not depend on the truth value of its component sentences—referred to as its antecedent and consequent, respectively.

So, how should we think of a conditional in strictly truth-functional terms? To begin with, we may assume that a conditional is true if both its antecedent and consequent are true. Moreover, we may assume that it is false if its antecedent is true and its consequent false. The harder cases to handle are those in which the antecedent is false. What truth value should we assign to the conditional in these cases? We will adopt the convention, odd as it may seem, that a conditional with a false antecedent is true. As we will see later, there is a proof theoretic rationale for this policy.

So, the semantic rule for conditionals is as follows:

(Conditional): For any sentences \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\): \(v^\sigma(\varphi\rightarrow\psi)=\mathbf{F}\) iff \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\) and \(v^\sigma(\psi)=\mathbf{F}\).

The truth table for \(P\rightarrow Q\) is as follows:

\(P\) \(Q\) \(P\rightarrow Q\)
\(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{F}\)
\(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{T}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)
\(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{F}\) \(\mathbf{T}\)

Notice that the truth conditions for the conditional \(P\rightarrow Q\) are exactly the same as the truth conditions for the sentence \(\neg(P\wedge\neg Q)\). Both are true just in case either \(P\) is false or \(Q\) is true (or both). This highlights the fact that the syntax of the language L\(_P\) now greatly exceeds its expressive capacity. That is, in the language, there are many distinct ways of saying the same thing.

When two sentences have exactly the same truth conditions, we say that they are logically equivalent.

Definition 2.8 Two sentences \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are logically equivalent, written \(\varphi\equiv \psi\), if, for any interpretation \(\sigma\), \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=v^\sigma(\psi)\).

Two other important semantic notions are those of logical truth and logical falsehood:

Definition 2.9 A sentence \(\varphi\) is a logical truth if, for every interpretation \(\sigma\): \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{T}\). A sentence \(\varphi\) is a logical falsehood if, for every interpretation \(\sigma\): \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=\mathbf{F}\).

In the context of propositional logic, logical truths are sometimes referred to as tautologies and logical falsehoods as contradiction. In fact, our final connective, the biconditional, allows us to express logical equivalence in terms of logical truth. The rule for the biconditional is as follows:

(Biconditional): For any sentences \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\): \(v^\sigma(\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi)=\mathbf{T}\) iff \(v^\sigma(\varphi)=v^\sigma(\psi)\).

Convince yourself that, for any sentences \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\), \(\varphi\equiv\psi\) iff the sentence \(\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi\) is a tautology.
